## Chapter 19

### If Hormuz is disrupted

Impact on energy, inflation, and markets

The global energy system is designed to flow.

Not to stop.

The Strait of Hormuz is one of the points where that distinction becomes critical.

A substantial share of the world’s oil passes through this passage¹.

There is no immediate alternative with equivalent capacity.

That makes any disruption a global event.

If Hormuz is disrupted, the impact is not gradual.

It is immediate.

The first effect appears in markets.

Oil prices do not wait for confirmation.

They react to signals.

To perceived risk.

To uncertainty.

Prices rise.

Not linearly.

Abruptly.

That increase does not remain confined to oil.

It propagates.

To fuels.

To transportation.

To production.

To costs.

The second effect is inflationary.

Energy is a cross-cutting input.

It affects the entire economic structure.

When its price rises, the impact spreads.

Production costs increase.

Consumer prices adjust.

Inflation accelerates.

The third effect is operational.

Supply chains are affected.

Not only by cost.

But by uncertainty.

Operators adjust routes.

Increase inventories.

Reconfigure decisions.

Flow does not disappear.

But it loses efficiency.

The fourth effect is financial.

Markets react.

Volatility increases.

Expectations shift.

Risk perception rises.

Investment slows.

Decisions are delayed.

The fifth effect is geopolitical.

Energy becomes an instrument of pressure.

Relationships among actors shift.

Policy decisions accelerate.

The system enters a phase of tension.

These effects do not occur sequentially.

They occur in parallel.

They reinforce each other.

They amplify the impact.

That is the critical point.

The disruption of Hormuz is not only an energy event.

It is a systemic event.

From a structural perspective, vulnerability lies in concentration.

High volumes of flow in a limited space.

Significant dependence without equivalent alternatives.

This creates immediate exposure.

From an adversarial perspective, the objective is not necessarily a full closure.

It is enough to generate uncertainty.

To raise perceived risk.

To introduce signals of instability.

That alone is sufficient to activate the system.

Prices react.

Markets adjust.

Impact begins.

Effective protection of Hormuz cannot rely only on response.

It must rely on anticipation.

First, continuous monitoring of signals.

Changes in regional activity.

Rising tensions.

Shifts in transit patterns.

Second, rapid response mechanisms.

The ability to act before perception becomes operational reality.

Third, development of alternatives.

Not to fully replace the flow.

But to reduce its concentration.

Fourth, international coordination.

Dependence is global.

The response must be as well.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they reduce its impact.

If Hormuz is disrupted, the global system does not stop.

But it changes.

It becomes more expensive.

Slower.

More uncertain.

And within that change, even if partial, lies the impact.

Because in an interdependent system, total collapse is not required to generate global consequences.

It is enough to alter the flow.

And at that critical, concentrated point, the margin for error is minimal.
