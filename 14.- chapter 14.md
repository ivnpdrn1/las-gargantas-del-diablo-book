## Chapter 14

### The Black Sea: the invisible chokepoint

Not all chokepoints are obvious.

Some are not perceived as such until they fail.

The Black Sea is one of them.

It is not a narrow passage in the traditional sense.

It is not a single route that concentrates all flow.

But it is a critical exit point.

A space where production, transport, and access to the global system converge.

The Black Sea functions as a functional chokepoint.

A node where flow is not constrained by immediate geography, but by access.

Access to markets.

Access to routes.

Access to stability.

From this region, a significant share of the world’s grains and fertilizers is exported¹.

Wheat.

Corn.

Vegetable oils.

Agricultural inputs.

These are not secondary goods.

They are essential.

They affect food security.

Global prices.

Social stability.

Unlike other chokepoints, where impact is quickly visible in energy markets, here it is visible in food.

And that impact follows a different dynamic.

It is not immediate.

But it is deep.

And socially sensitive.

The vulnerabilities of the Black Sea are not defined by narrowness.

They are defined by dependence.

First, export concentration.

A large volume of production depends on this outlet.

Second, access dependency.

Flow depends on security and regional stability.

Third, food market sensitivity.

Small changes in supply generate significant price movements.

Fourth, interconnection with other routes.

What happens here does not stay here.

It transmits globally.

The Black Sea does not need to be fully closed to generate impact.

It is enough to constrain operations.

Reduce flow.

Introduce uncertainty.

That alone is sufficient to affect markets.

Prices react.

Countries adjust reserves.

Policies shift.

Impact begins before total disruption.

From an adversarial perspective, this makes it a strategic point.

Not because of its visibility.

But because of its effect.

There is no need to intervene at scale.

It is enough to affect continuity.

The system responds.

Not only to what happens.

But to what might happen.

That is the mechanism.

Perception becomes operational.

Current capabilities allow for monitoring the region.

But effective protection requires more than observation.

It requires stability.

It requires agreements.

It requires mechanisms that ensure continuity of flow even under adverse conditions.

A proactive doctrine in this context involves several elements.

First, securing export routes.

Ensuring access to global markets.

Second, diversifying export points.

Reducing concentration.

Third, international coordination mechanisms.

The impact is global.

The response must be as well.

Fourth, continuous monitoring of pressure signals.

Changes in production.

Variations in prices.

Disruptions in logistics.

These measures do not eliminate vulnerability.

But they reduce its impact.

The Black Sea represents a different kind of chokepoint.

Not defined by physical narrowness.

But by systemic function.

A point where production becomes flow.

And where any disruption affects global stability.

To understand this chokepoint is to understand that risk is not limited to energy or trade.

It extends to food.

And when food is affected, consequences move beyond economics.

They become social.

And at that point, the impact takes on a different dimension.
