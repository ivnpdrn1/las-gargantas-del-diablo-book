## Chapter 14

### The Taiwan Strait: the chokepoint of the 21st century

Semiconductors, trade, and deterrence

The global system has changed.

For decades, energy defined its stability.

Today, technology does.

And in that shift, a new chokepoint emerges.

The Taiwan Strait.

It is not the narrowest.

It is not the most heavily trafficked.

But it is one of the most critical.

Because at this point, it is not only trade that flows.

Capacity is concentrated.

Industrial capacity.

Technological capacity.

Innovative capacity.

The Taiwan Strait connects one of the most dynamic regions in the world to the global system.

A significant share of international trade passes through its waters¹.

But its importance goes beyond transit.

It sits at the center of the most important technological supply chain of the 21st century.

Semiconductors.

Electronic components.

Systems that sustain the digital economy.

From automotive manufacturing to artificial intelligence.

The production of these components is highly concentrated.

And a substantial portion of that concentration is located in Taiwan.

That is the critical point.

This is not only a route.

It is a node of capacity.

Unlike other chokepoints, where impact is measured in flow, here it is measured in production.

Without semiconductors, the system does not stop immediately.

But it loses continuity.

Factories slow.

Products are delayed.

Innovation decelerates.

The impact is less visible.

But deeper.

And more prolonged.

The vulnerabilities of this chokepoint are distinct.

First, industrial concentration.

A limited geography sustains a significant share of global production.

Second, geopolitical tension.

The region is strategically sensitive.

Third, technological dependence.

There are no immediate substitutes at scale.

Fourth, supply chain complexity.

Multiple interconnected stages.

This creates a different kind of risk.

Not based only on disruption of transit.

But on loss of capacity.

From an adversarial perspective, this changes the logic.

It is not necessary to fully block the strait.

It is enough to affect stability.

To introduce uncertainty.

To undermine confidence in production continuity.

That alone is sufficient to generate impact.

Companies adjust inventories.

Governments revise policies.

Markets react.

Impact begins before any physical disruption.

This is the new paradigm.

Disruption is not only logistical.

It is structural.

The system does not respond only to interruption of flow.

It responds to the possibility of losing capacity.

Current capabilities allow monitoring of transit and production.

But protecting this chokepoint requires a different approach.

It is not only about securing passage.

It is about securing continuity.

A proactive doctrine involves several elements.

First, diversification.

Reducing concentration of production.

Second, technological redundancy.

Developing alternative capabilities.

Third, international coordination.

Dependence is global.

The response must be as well.

Fourth, monitoring of signals.

Changes in the political environment.

Variations in production.

Indicators of risk.

These measures do not eliminate vulnerability.

But they reduce it.

The Taiwan Strait represents the evolution of the global system.

From physical to systemic.

From logistical to industrial.

To understand this chokepoint is to understand the future of global risk.

Because in the 21st century, it is not enough for energy to flow.

Technology must flow as well.

And when that technology is concentrated in a single location, the system becomes vulnerable.

Not to immediate interruption.

But to loss of continuity.

And within that loss, even if partial, lies the greatest risk.
