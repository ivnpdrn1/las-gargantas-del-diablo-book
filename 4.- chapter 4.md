## Chapter 4

### Adversarial Thinking

How someone seeking to disrupt the global system would think

The global system must not only be understood; it must also be analyzed from the perspective of those who seek to disrupt it. Most traditional approaches rely on a defensive logic: they describe how the system functions, how it is organized, and how it maintains equilibrium. That view, however, is incomplete. A system is only truly understood when it has also been examined from the point at which it can break.

Adopting an adversarial mindset does not mean justifying disruption. It means anticipating it. It requires temporarily setting aside the logic of protection and embracing the logic of impact. From that perspective, the objective is not to control the entire system, but to identify where limited actions can generate disproportionate consequences.

The modern global system is defined by efficiency. That efficiency is achieved through concentrated flows, optimized routes, and reduced redundancies. What appears as strength from within can be perceived as vulnerability from the outside. Wherever flow is constant and concentrated, any disruption carries systemic relevance.

Adversarial thinking therefore focuses on identifying these critical points. Not all locations have equal strategic value. A chokepoint is not merely a narrow passage; it is a node where dependence, volume, and limited alternatives converge. From this standpoint, the central question is not where to strike, but where minimal effort would produce maximum effect.

An adversary does not require total superiority or unlimited resources. Their advantage lies in selection. A localized action, executed at the right place and at the right time, can trigger a chain reaction that extends far beyond the initial point of disruption. The impact is not confined to the physical interruption of flow; it extends to risk perception, market reactions, and the reconfiguration of logistical and political decisions.

In this context, uncertainty becomes a strategic tool. It is not necessary to completely shut down a chokepoint to alter its function. It is enough to introduce doubt about its reliability. Once confidence in a route declines, actors begin to adjust: they reroute shipments, increase inventories, raise costs, and shift risk across the system.

The result is cumulative pressure. Each individual decision is rational in isolation, but collectively they generate systemic tension. The flow does not disappear, but it becomes more expensive, slower, and less predictable. This gradual degradation is often more effective than a sudden disruption.

Adversarial thinking also emphasizes sequencing. A single event may be absorbed if the system retains sufficient response capacity. However, a series of smaller, distributed events can overwhelm that capacity. The system does not fail because of an initial shock; it fails due to accumulated stress that exceeds its ability to adapt.

Another key element is asymmetry. The tools required to generate disruption are not always complex or costly. Accessible technologies, unconventional means, and limited actions can achieve strategic significance when applied in high-sensitivity contexts. The relationship between effort and impact is not linear; it becomes exponential when directed at critical nodes.

From this perspective, chokepoints are no longer just physical infrastructures. They are strategic decision spaces. Their value lies not only in what they enable, but in what would happen if they cease to function normally. That possibility defines their true importance.

Understanding this logic requires acknowledging that the global system is not designed to operate under sustained pressure. It is optimized for efficiency under stable conditions. When those conditions shift, its capacity to adapt depends on factors that have not always been prioritized: redundancy, coordination, and anticipation.

Effective protection, therefore, cannot rely solely on presence or visible control. It must begin with a deep understanding of how an adversary perceives the system. Identifying vulnerabilities from within is necessary; recognizing them from the outside is essential.

This shift in perspective transforms how risk is analyzed. It is no longer sufficient to assess probabilities; one must understand dynamics. It is no longer enough to react to events; one must anticipate scenarios. And above all, it is not about protecting everything, but about protecting what, if disrupted, would destabilize the whole.

Adversarial thinking does not redefine the system. It redefines how it must be defended. Because in an interconnected world, the greatest vulnerability is not the lack of capability, but the lack of foresight.
