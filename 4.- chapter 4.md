## Chapter 4

### The domino effect

How a local event becomes a global crisis

The global system does not collapse all at once.

It unravels in sequence.

A local event rarely remains local.

In an interdependent system, every point is connected.

Every flow depends on another.

Every decision affects multiple nodes.

That is the principle of the domino effect.

A disruption at a chokepoint does not stay contained.

It propagates.

It amplifies.

It becomes something larger.

The first level of the domino effect is immediate.

A disruption alters flow.

A delay.

A restriction.

An increase in perceived risk.

The system reacts.

Operators adjust routes.

Markets respond.

Decisions accelerate.

That is the beginning.

The second level is operational.

A change in one point creates pressure on others.

Alternative routes receive more traffic.

Capacity tightens.

Efficiency declines.

The system absorbs the shock.

But under strain.

The third level is economic.

Costs increase.

Not only because of the initial disruption.

But because of cumulative effects.

More distance.

More time.

More resources.

Those increases propagate.

Through supply chains.

Through prices.

Through inflation.

The fourth level is systemic.

Decisions begin to interact.

What one actor does affects others.

Routes become congested.

Transit times become unpredictable.

Planning loses precision.

The system no longer behaves in a stable way.

The fifth level is structural.

Confidence is affected.

And when confidence declines, the system changes.

Actors become more conservative.

They increase inventories.

They reduce exposure.

They adjust strategies.

That behavior modifies flow.

And amplifies the initial impact.

That is the critical point.

The domino effect does not depend only on the event.

It depends on how the system reacts.

A small disruption can generate a large impact.

Not because of its size.

But because of its propagation.

From a structural perspective, this occurs because of interdependence.

Chokepoints do not operate in isolation.

They form a network.

And in a network, any change propagates.

The speed of that propagation is critical.

In highly optimized systems, it is rapid.

Because margins are thin.

There is little room to absorb variation.

From an adversarial perspective, the domino effect is a multiplier.

There is no need to create a large event.

A small disruption at the right point can trigger the chain.

And the system will amplify the impact.

That is the mechanism.

Not total disruption.

But activation of the chain.

Current capabilities allow response to events.

But effective protection requires anticipating propagation.

Not only the initial disruption.

A proactive doctrine involves several elements.

First, identifying critical nodes.

Points where impact amplifies.

Second, monitoring early signals.

Indicators of pressure.

Changes in flow.

Variations in cost.

Third, acting before saturation.

Preventing the system from entering a degradation phase.

Fourth, coordinating responses.

The domino effect cannot be managed in isolation.

It requires collective action.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they reduce its propagation.

The domino effect is the internal logic of the global system.

It explains why local events generate global consequences.

And why stability depends not only on avoiding disruptions.

But on managing how they spread.

Because in an interconnected system, risk does not reside only at the point of origin.

It resides in the chain.

And in that chain, every link matters.
