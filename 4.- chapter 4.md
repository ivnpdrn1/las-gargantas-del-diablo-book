## Chapter 4

### Adversarial Thinking

How someone seeking to disrupt the global system would think

The global system must not only be understood; it must also be analyzed from the perspective of those who seek to disrupt it. Most traditional approaches rely on a defensive logic: they describe how the system functions, how it is organized, and how it maintains equilibrium. That view is necessary, but incomplete. A system is fully understood only when it is examined from the point at which it can break.

Adopting an adversarial perspective does not imply justifying disruption; it means anticipating it. It requires moving beyond the question of how to protect the system and asking how it could be perturbed. From that standpoint, the objective is not to control the entire system, but to identify where limited actions can produce disproportionate effects.

The contemporary global system is designed for efficiency. Concentrated flows, optimized routes, and reduced redundancies enable speed and lower costs. Yet those same features create exposure. Wherever flow is constant and concentrated, any disturbance gains systemic relevance.

Adversarial thinking therefore focuses on identifying these critical points. Not all locations carry the same strategic value. A chokepoint is a node where volume, dependence, and the lack of immediate alternatives converge. From this perspective, the key question is not where to intervene, but where minimal effort would yield maximum effect.

An adversary does not require total superiority or unlimited resources. Their advantage lies in selection. A localized action, executed at the right place and at the right time, can trigger a chain of reactions that far exceeds the initial event. The impact extends beyond the physical interruption of flow; it reaches risk perception, market reactions, and the reconfiguration of logistical and political decisions.

In this context, uncertainty becomes a strategic instrument. It is not necessary to fully close a chokepoint to affect its function. It is enough to introduce doubt about its stability. As confidence in a route declines, actors adjust: they reroute shipments, increase inventories, raise costs, and shift risk across the system.

The result is cumulative pressure. Each decision is rational in isolation, yet together they generate growing systemic tension. Flow does not disappear, but it becomes more expensive, slower, and less predictable. This progressive degradation can be more effective than a sudden disruption.

Adversarial thinking also emphasizes sequencing. A single event may be absorbed if the system retains response capacity. However, a series of smaller, distributed events can overwhelm that capacity. The system does not fail because of an initial shock, but because accumulated pressures exceed its ability to adapt.

Another central element is asymmetry. The means required to generate disruption are not always complex or costly. Limited tools, applied in high-sensitivity contexts, can produce amplified effects. The relationship between effort and impact is not linear; it becomes exponential when directed at critical points¹.

From this perspective, chokepoints are no longer just physical infrastructures; they are strategic decision spaces. Their value lies not only in what they enable, but in what would happen if they cease to function normally. That possibility defines their true importance.

Understanding this logic requires acknowledging that the global system is not designed to operate under sustained pressure. It is optimized for stable conditions. When those conditions shift, its capacity to adapt depends on factors that have not always been prioritized: redundancy, coordination, and anticipation².

Effective protection, therefore, cannot rely solely on presence or visible control. It must begin with a deep understanding of how an adversary perceives the system. Identifying vulnerabilities from within is necessary; recognizing them from the outside is essential.

This shift in perspective transforms how risk is analyzed. It is not enough to assess probabilities; one must understand dynamics. It is not enough to react to events; one must anticipate scenarios. And above all, it is not about protecting everything, but about protecting what, if disrupted, would have systemic consequences.

Adversarial thinking does not redefine the system; it redefines how it must be defended. Because in an interconnected environment, the greatest vulnerability is not the lack of capability, but the lack of foresight.
