## Chapter 3

### Interdependence: The Illusion of Stability

The global system functions because it is connected.

That is the premise.

Interdependence has been presented as a form of stability. The more connected economies are, the harder it is for the system to fail.

That logic has guided decisions for decades.

Integration.
Optimization.
Expansion of networks.

All under the same idea.

More connection implies more resilience.

But that idea is incomplete.

And in certain contexts, misleading.

Interdependence does not eliminate risk.

It redistributes it.

And in some cases, it concentrates it.

When multiple systems depend on the same flows, those flows stop being just routes.

They become conditions of operation.

The system does not only use them.

It depends on them.

That dependence is not visible under normal conditions.

As long as flow continues, the system appears stable.

But that stability is not structural.

It is operational.

It functions only as long as continuity is uninterrupted.

The problem is that continuity is not guaranteed.

An interdependent system can absorb small variations.

But it is not designed to absorb sustained disruptions at critical points.

And when those points coincide with chokepoints, the impact is amplified.

Interdependence creates efficiency.

But it also synchronizes vulnerabilities.

When one part of the system is affected, others do not remain isolated.

They adjust.

They slow down.

They become strained.

Not because of geographic proximity.

But because of functional dependence.

A local event becomes a global signal.

Markets react.
Supply chains reconfigure.
Political decisions accelerate.

Everything happens in parallel.

Interdependence does not create distance between events.

It reduces it.

That is why the idea that the system is stable because it is connected must be reconsidered.

Connection does not eliminate risk.

It makes it faster.

More immediate.

More difficult to contain.

In an interdependent system, time compresses.

What once took weeks now happens in days.

What was once gradual becomes abrupt.

The margin for response shrinks.

And that is the critical point.

Because stability does not depend only on the structure of the system.

It depends on the time available to respond.

When that time disappears, stability becomes fragile.

Interdependence also introduces another effect.

The illusion of substitution.

It is assumed that if one flow fails, another can replace it.

That if one route is disrupted, an alternative exists.

But in many cases, those alternatives are limited.

Or more costly.

Or simply unavailable at the required scale.

The capacity for substitution is not immediate.

And in that interval, the system absorbs pressure.

That pressure translates into prices.

Into scarcity.

Into accelerated decision-making.

Interdependence does not eliminate constraints.

It makes them shared.

And when they are shared, the impact is simultaneous.

Not sequential.

That is the fundamental shift.

The system no longer fails in parts.

It fails together.

Not because everything collapses at once.

But because everything reacts at once.

That simultaneous reaction is what turns a disruption into a crisis.

And this dynamic is especially visible in chokepoints.

Because they are the places where interdependence becomes tangible.

Where multiple flows converge.

Where multiple dependencies intersect.

And where a single disruption affects all.

Stability, therefore, does not depend only on the existence of flows.

It depends on their ability to continue under pressure.

And that is not always guaranteed.

The global system has been designed to maximize efficiency.

Not to maximize resilience.

Interdependence has been the means to achieve that.

But in the process, it has reduced the system’s capacity to absorb disruption.

The result is a system that performs well under normal conditions.

But that can become unstable under adverse conditions.

Not because it is weak.

But because it is sensitive.

That sensitivity is the cost of efficiency.

And that is the central paradox.

The same mechanism that allows the system to function smoothly is the one that exposes it to high-impact disruption.

Understanding this paradox is essential.

Because it allows the question to change.

It is not about how to make the system more efficient.

It is about how to make it more resilient.

And that difference redefines how chokepoints must be analyzed.

Not only as transit points.

But as locations where interdependence concentrates.

And where, therefore, risk is amplified.

The next step is to understand how that risk becomes impact.

How a disruption at one point turns into a chain reaction.

How a local event stops being local.

That is the domino effect.

And it is the mechanism that connects vulnerability to crisis.
