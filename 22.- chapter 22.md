## Chapter 22

### Simultaneous crises

When multiple chokepoints fail at the same time

The global system is not designed to absorb multiple disruptions simultaneously.

It is designed to optimize flows under stable conditions.

When a single chokepoint fails, the system adapts.

It reroutes flows.

It adjusts costs.

It reconfigures decisions.

But when several chokepoints fail at the same time, that adaptive capacity declines.

And the system enters a different dynamic.

Not one of adaptation.

But of saturation.

Simultaneous crises are not the sum of individual events.

They are multipliers.

Each disruption amplifies the others.

The alternatives that normally absorb pressure become saturated.

Secondary routes become congested.

Transit times extend.

Costs rise.

Flow continues.

But under critical conditions.

The first effect is logistical.

Rerouting overlaps.

Distances increase.

Absorption capacity declines.

Systems operate at their limits.

The second effect is economic.

Costs do not rise linearly.

They spike.

Because competition for resources intensifies.

Fuel.

Transport capacity.

Infrastructure.

All become scarcer.

The third effect is operational.

Planning loses precision.

Decisions become reactive.

Margins for error disappear.

The system loses fine control.

The fourth effect is financial.

Uncertainty increases.

Markets react with greater volatility.

Expectations diverge.

Investment decisions are delayed.

The fifth effect is systemic.

The system no longer behaves as a set of independent flows.

It behaves as a stressed network.

A change in one node affects multiple others.

Disruptions propagate.

They reinforce each other.

They amplify.

That is the critical point.

Simultaneity transforms the nature of risk.

From a structural perspective, vulnerability lies in interdependence.

Chokepoints do not operate in isolation.

They are part of a system.

And when that system faces multiple pressures, its response capacity diminishes.

From an adversarial perspective, this scenario is particularly relevant.

A large-scale event in a single point is not required.

Multiple smaller disruptions across different points can generate greater impact.

That is the mechanism.

Distribute pressure.

Saturate adaptive capacity.

Force the system to operate outside equilibrium.

Current capabilities are designed to respond to individual events.

But they are limited when facing simultaneous crises.

Coordination becomes more complex.

Response speed declines.

Decisions fragment.

A proactive doctrine in this context requires a different approach.

First, systemic visibility.

Understanding interdependencies.

Second, global coordination capacity.

Response cannot remain local.

Third, development of redundancies.

Not in a single point.

But across the system.

Fourth, monitoring of multiple signals.

Not analyzing events in isolation.

But as part of a broader pattern.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they increase absorption capacity.

When multiple chokepoints fail simultaneously, the system does not collapse immediately.

But it enters a stress phase.

It becomes slower.

More expensive.

More uncertain.

And in that state, stability declines.

To understand simultaneous crises is to understand the limits of the global system.

Because risk is not defined by the intensity of a single event.

It is defined by the interaction of multiple events.

And within that interaction lies the greatest vulnerability.
