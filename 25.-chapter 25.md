## Chapter 25

### What governments must do now

Concrete actions to reduce vulnerability

The global system no longer faces a theoretical risk.

The risk is real.

It is recurring.

And it is increasing.

The question is not if the next disruption will occur.

It is when.

And how prepared systems will be to respond.

Governments cannot eliminate vulnerability.

But they can reduce it.

And above all, they can manage its impact.

That requires moving from diagnosis to action.

From analysis to execution.

The first step is recognizing the nature of risk.

It is not local.

It is not isolated.

It is systemic.

And therefore, responses cannot be fragmented.

The second step is prioritization.

Not all chokepoints carry the same level of impact.

Not all require the same level of protection.

Identifying critical nodes is essential.

Energy.

Key trade routes.

Strategic infrastructure.

Technological capacity.

These are the points where intervention has the greatest effect.

The third step is investment.

Resilience has a cost.

Infrastructure.

Technology.

Response capacity.

These investments do not always produce visible short-term returns.

But they define long-term stability.

The fourth step is information integration.

Without full visibility, there is no anticipation.

Governments must build systems that integrate data.

That connect actors.

That enable early signal detection.

The fifth step is strengthening coordination.

No country operates in isolation.

The system is interdependent.

Responses must be as well.

Coordination is not optional.

It is structural.

The sixth step is building response capacity.

Speed.

Precision.

Flexibility.

Fast response reduces impact.

Delayed response amplifies it.

The seventh step is anticipation.

Acting before the event.

Not waiting for confirmation.

Not waiting for complete evidence.

Proactivity does not eliminate risk.

But it prevents propagation.

The eighth step is diversification.

Reducing concentration.

Increasing alternatives.

Not eliminating dependencies.

But managing them.

The ninth step is strengthening trust.

The global system depends on expectations.

On perceived stability.

When confidence erodes, impact amplifies.

Maintaining that confidence is part of resilience.

The tenth step is adaptation.

The system evolves.

Threats evolve.

Responses must evolve as well.

There is no permanent solution.

There is only adaptive capacity.

These actions do not eliminate risk.

But they change its nature.

From critical vulnerability.

To manageable risk.

From a strategic perspective, the greatest error is inaction.

Waiting.

Delaying.

Assuming the system will continue to function as it has.

That is the real risk.

From an adversarial perspective, lack of preparation is an opportunity.

There is no need to create vulnerability.

It already exists.

It only needs to be exploited.

Governments have a central role.

Not as the only actors.

But as coordinators.

As integrators.

As stewards of stability.

Resilience is not an option.

It is a necessity.

Acting now does not guarantee the absence of crisis.

But it guarantees something more important.

The capacity to respond.

And in an interdependent system, that capacity defines the difference between disruption and collapse.
