## Prologue

### Before It Happens

Crises rarely begin where everyone is looking.

They begin where attention is weakest.

Not in the headlines.
Not in the visible centers of power.
Not in the places that dominate public conversation.

They begin at the points where the system becomes narrow.

For decades, the world has operated under an implicit assumption: as long as the flow continues, the system works.

Energy, food, manufacturing, trade.

Everything moves.

And as long as it moves, stability is assumed.

But that stability is not a guarantee.

It is a fragile condition.

A condition sustained by the uninterrupted operation of processes that depend on specific, limited, and vulnerable locations.

Recent history has shown that complex systems do not fail gradually.

They fail abruptly.

And when they do, the impact is not local.

It propagates.

It moves through markets.
It reshapes political decisions.
It alters economic priorities.

And it does so at a speed that exceeds the ability to respond.

The problem is not a lack of information.

It never has been.

The problem is how that information is interpreted.

Most analyses focus on extreme scenarios.

Visible events.
Total disruptions.
Declared conflicts.

But critical systems are not primarily compromised by those events.

They are compromised by something more subtle.

More gradual.
More difficult to detect.

The accumulation of pressure at the right points.

At those points, small disruptions generate disproportionate effects.

Not because they are large.

But because they occur in the right place.

This book does not emerge from theoretical concern.

It emerges from observation.

The global system, as currently designed, prioritizes efficiency.

It reduces redundancy.
It optimizes cost.
It minimizes friction.

But in doing so, it also reduces its ability to absorb disruption.

The consequence is clear.

A highly efficient system can also be highly vulnerable.

The world’s chokepoints are not a secret.

They are known.
They are visible.
They have been studied.

But they have not been treated with the urgency their importance demands.

This book proposes a different way to see them.

Not as routes.
Not as geographic features.

But as points of decision.

Places where global stability can be maintained—or broken.

The goal is not to create alarm.

It is to create anticipation.

Because the real risk is not that a crisis occurs.

It is that it occurs without preparation.

Thinking about these points requires a shift in perspective.

It requires moving beyond the question of what happens if something fails.

And asking instead what must be done now to ensure it does not.

The difference between reaction and anticipation is not theoretical.

It is practical.

It is the difference between containing a crisis and suffering its consequences.

This prologue is not a warning.

It is a starting point.

An invitation to look at the system from where it is truly vulnerable.

Because in an interconnected world, what seems distant can become immediate.

And what appears stable can depend on a single point.

Before it happens.
