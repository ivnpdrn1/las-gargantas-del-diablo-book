## **Introduction**

### **The World We Think We Understand**

The world seems to work.

Products arrive.
Energy flows.
Markets react, but do not collapse.
Systems, at least on the surface, hold together.

This is the dominant perception: continuity.

But that perception is an illusion.

Behind this apparent stability lies a different reality.

The global system is not evenly distributed. It does not depend on a balanced network. It is not as resilient as we assume.

It depends on points.

Specific points.
Limited points.
Vulnerable points.

Places where the flow of the world concentrates.

These points—known as chokepoints—are not merely trade routes.

They are critical structures.

They are the narrow passages through which the global economy breathes.

And like any narrow passage, they can be pressured.

The fundamental mistake of the current system is not that it ignores these points.

It is that it underestimates their fragility.

For decades, uninterrupted flow has created a false sense of security. What has not stopped is assumed not to stop.

But the history of complex systems suggests otherwise:

They do not fail when they are weak. They fail when they are pressed in the right place.

This book begins from a different premise.

It does not analyze the world as a network of countries or economies.

It analyzes it as a system of flows.

And within that system, it identifies the locations where those flows can be altered with the greatest impact.

Not all risks are equal.

Some require vast resources.
Others require time.
Some demand direct confrontation.

But there is a type of risk that operates differently.

The one that exploits concentration.

The one that does not need to destroy. It only needs to interrupt.

The Strait of Hormuz, the Red Sea, the Strait of Malacca, the Panama Canal, the Taiwan Strait, the Black Sea.

Each of these places shares a defining characteristic:

Their importance exceeds their size.

What happens within them does not remain there.

It propagates.

It affects energy.
It affects food.
It affects manufacturing.
It affects political decisions.

And it does so immediately.

Yet the problem is not only the existence of these points.

It is how we think about them.

Most analyses focus on extreme scenarios.

What happens if they are closed.
What happens if war breaks out.

But that is not the primary risk.

The real risk lies in the space in between.

In partial disruption.
In sustained pressure.
In uncertainty.

In actions that are not large enough to trigger a full-scale response, yet effective enough to strain the system.

This book proposes a shift in perspective.

From reaction to anticipation.
From description to action.
From geography to strategy.

It is not enough to understand where chokepoints are located.

It is necessary to understand how they can be used to generate disruption, what vulnerabilities make them critical, what capabilities exist today, and—most importantly—what does not yet exist.

The central question is not theoretical.

It is operational.

What must be done now to prevent a disruption in these points from producing global consequences?

Answering that question requires a different way of thinking.

It requires considering not only how the system functions, but how it could be targeted.

Not from the perspective of total destruction, but from the logic of efficient disruption.

Because the global system does not need to be destroyed to fail.

It only needs to lose stability.

This book does not aim to create alarm.

It aims to create clarity.

It does not propose unlikely scenarios.

It analyzes existing vulnerabilities.

It does not stop at describing risks.

It defines actions.

In the pages that follow, each chokepoint will be examined not only for its importance, but for its vulnerability.

Not only for what it is, but for what it could become if no action is taken.

Because in an interdependent world, stability is not permanent.

It is a condition that must be protected.

And that protection does not begin when a crisis occurs.

It begins before.

At the points where the system becomes narrow.
