## Chapter 10

### Proactivity vs. reaction

Why being late is the greatest strategic risk

The global system responds.

But almost always after the fact.

After the event.

After the initial impact.

After flow has already been altered.

That is the problem.

The difference between proactivity and reaction is not speed.

It is timing.

Acting before.

Or acting after.

And in an interdependent system, that moment changes everything.

Reaction is visible.

Resources are mobilized.

Actors coordinate.

Measures are implemented.

But all of it happens once the damage has begun.

When the domino effect is already in motion.

Proactivity is different.

It is quiet.

It happens before the event.

Before the system is affected.

And that is why it is harder.

Because it requires anticipation.

Not evidence.

The first problem with reaction is delay.

The time between the event and the response allows the impact to amplify.

Flows are disrupted.

Markets react.

Decisions shift.

And by the time the response arrives, the system has already changed.

The second problem is escalation.

A delayed response requires more resources.

More coordination.

More effort.

The cost of intervention increases.

The system absorbs not only the initial disruption.

But also the cost of reacting to it.

The third problem is loss of control.

In a reactive phase, the system operates under pressure.

Decisions are urgent.

Precision declines.

Margins for error shrink.

The system loses stability.

The fourth problem is repetition.

Reaction does not always address the cause.

It addresses the effect.

This allows risk to persist.

Events to recur.

Exposure to remain.

Proactivity faces its own challenges.

First, uncertainty.

It is not always clear when to act.

Second, cost.

Acting early requires investment without guaranteed events.

Third, coordination.

It demands alignment among actors.

But despite these challenges, proactivity has a critical advantage.

It reduces propagation.

Acting early prevents the system from entering a degradation phase.

It stops the domino effect from activating.

It limits amplification.

From a structural perspective, the current system favors reaction.

Because it responds to what is visible.

What is immediate.

But real risk builds beforehand.

In signals.

In gradual shifts.

In indicators.

From an adversarial perspective, this is exploitable.

A reactive system always arrives late.

It acts only after impact begins.

That maximizes effect.

That is the mechanism.

Not accelerating the event.

But delaying the response.

Current capabilities allow reaction.

But not always anticipation.

That is the limit.

An effective doctrine must reverse this logic.

First, monitor early signals.

Identify changes before events occur.

Second, enable anticipatory decision-making.

Act with incomplete information.

Third, coordinate in advance.

Align actors before crisis.

Fourth, adapt continuously.

Adjust strategies in real time.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they reduce its impact.

The difference between proactivity and reaction is not technical.

It is strategic.

It determines whether the system absorbs disruption.

Or amplifies it.

Because in an interdependent environment, time is not neutral.

Time determines the scale of impact.

And arriving late is not an operational mistake.

It is the greatest strategic risk.
