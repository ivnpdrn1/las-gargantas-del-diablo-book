## Chapter 10

### Malacca: Asia’s chokepoint

The global system does not depend only on energy.

It depends on flow.

And in Asia, that flow converges in one place.

The Strait of Malacca.

One of the busiest maritime corridors in the world¹.

A narrow passage.

With massive impact.

Malacca connects the Indian Ocean to the Pacific.

It connects the Middle East to Asia.

It connects producers to consumers.

But above all, it connects energy to industry.

Countries such as China, Japan, and South Korea rely on this route to secure the flow of oil, gas, and essential goods.

It is not an option.

It is a necessity.

The volume that passes through Malacca is not only high.

It is critical.

And that criticality is not defined only by quantity.

It is defined by dependence.

Unlike other chokepoints where partial alternatives exist, Malacca concentrates a large share of Asia’s flow into a single passage.

This creates structural exposure.

If the flow is disrupted, the impact does not disperse.

It concentrates.

The vulnerabilities of Malacca are clear.

First, traffic density.

Large vessels move through a constrained space, increasing the system’s sensitivity to any incident.

Second, limited maneuverability.

The strait presents conditions that restrict rapid rerouting or operational adjustments.

Third, proximity to multiple jurisdictions.

Coordination among actors is not always immediate or uniform.

Fourth, energy dependence.

A substantial portion of Asia’s energy supply passes through this point.

This amplifies any disruption.

The impact of a disruption in Malacca does not appear as immediate collapse.

It appears as pressure.

Flow continues.

But with friction.

Transit times extend.

Costs increase.

Decisions adjust.

And that pressure propagates.

To industrial production.

To energy prices.

To regional economic stability.

From a strategic perspective, Malacca is not only a chokepoint.

It is a multiplier.

Any disruption at this point is amplified by the scale of the system that depends on it.

From an adversarial perspective, this makes it a high-value target.

Not because of its physical fragility.

But because of its systemic sensitivity.

It is not necessary to close the strait.

It is enough to introduce uncertainty.

An incident.

A threat.

A signal of risk.

That is sufficient to trigger reactions.

Insurance premiums rise.

Operators reconsider routes.

Markets react.

Impact begins before any actual interruption.

That is the mechanism.

The system does not wait for confirmation.

It anticipates.

Current capabilities allow for traffic monitoring and incident management.

But effective protection of Malacca requires more than observation.

It requires anticipation.

It requires regional coordination.

It requires response mechanisms that operate at the speed of the system.

A proactive doctrine for Malacca involves several elements.

Full visibility of the flow.

Not only of vessels.

But of the factors that affect their movement.

Early identification of pressure signals.

Changes in navigation patterns.

Increases in transit times.

Variations in cost.

Coordination among regional actors.

The ability to act jointly and rapidly.

Development of alternatives.

Not to fully replace the flow.

But to reduce its concentration.

These measures do not eliminate vulnerability.

But they reduce its impact.

Malacca is not just a passage.

It is a convergence point.

Where energy, trade, and industry intersect.

And where any disruption can propagate at regional and global scale.

To understand Malacca is to understand Asia’s dependence.

And, by extension, the vulnerability of the global system.

Because when a single point concentrates so much flow, it does not need to fail completely to generate impact.

It only needs to stop functioning as expected.

And in that deviation, however small, lies the risk.
