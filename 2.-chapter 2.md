## Chapter 2

### The Concept of a Chokepoint

The world does not only depend on points.

It depends on points that can be controlled.

That is the next level of understanding.

It is not enough to know that global flows concentrate. It is necessary to understand that, when they do, they also become vulnerable to intervention.

That is the principle of the chokepoint.

A chokepoint is not simply a narrow place.

It is a point where flow can be influenced.

Where movement does not just pass, but can be altered.

And that possibility changes its nature.

From route to leverage.

The concept is not new.

It has existed for as long as routes have existed.

Since the earliest exchanges, mandatory passages have defined who controls access, who regulates movement, and who has the ability to interrupt it.

But in the modern global system, this concept takes on a different dimension.

It is no longer about regional trade.

It is about global interdependence.

A chokepoint is not defined solely by its geography.

It is defined by its function.

A narrow passage without flow is irrelevant.

A wide passage with high concentration of traffic can become critical.

For that reason, the true definition is not physical.

It is systemic.

A chokepoint is any point where the interruption of flow generates a disproportionate impact.

That impact does not depend on the size of the place.

It depends on the system’s level of dependence.

The most common mistake is to think of chokepoints as geographic accidents.

As natural features of land or sea.

But that is only part of the story.

In reality, many chokepoints are the result of human decisions.

Designed infrastructure.
Optimized routes.
Concentrated processes.

The global system does not only inherit its bottlenecks.

It creates them.

Efficiency pushes toward concentration.

Concentration creates dependence.

And dependence generates vulnerability.

This process is not visible in daily operations.

But it defines how the system behaves under pressure.

When everything works, chokepoints disappear into the background.

They are not perceived.

They do not attract attention.

But when something changes, they emerge immediately.

At that moment, they become visible.

Not because of their size.

But because of their impact.

A chokepoint does not need to be fully closed to affect the system.

It is enough to alter its functioning.

Reduce speed.
Increase uncertainty.
Raise cost.

The system responds immediately.

It does not wait for confirmation.

It reacts to signals.

That is why the power of a chokepoint does not lie in total blockage.

It lies in its ability to generate pressure.

That pressure can be physical.

But it can also be economic.

Or even psychological.

Markets react before events fully unfold.

Decisions change before flows actually stop.

That is the true scope of the concept.

A chokepoint is not only a transit point.

It is a point of influence.

And like any point of influence, it can be used.

From a strategic perspective, this changes everything.

Because it means that control does not require occupation.

Disruption does not require destruction.

Impact does not require full-scale action.

It only requires understanding where to act.

Chokepoints, therefore, are not only vulnerabilities.

They are tools.

Tools that can be used to protect the system.

Or to disrupt it.

It depends on who understands their value first.

This book analyzes chokepoints from that perspective.

Not as passive elements.

But as active points within a dynamic structure.

Each one represents a different combination of value, vulnerability, and potential for intervention.

And in every case, they share a common characteristic.

Their impact exceeds their visibility.

For that reason, understanding them is not an academic exercise.

It is a strategic necessity.

Because in a system where flow defines stability, the points where that flow can be altered define risk.

And understanding that risk is the first step toward anticipating it.
