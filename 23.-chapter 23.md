## Chapter 23

### Redundancy: myth or solution?

Alternative routes, energy, and real limits

The global system was designed for efficiency.

Not for redundancy.

For decades, optimization has guided decisions.

Reducing costs.

Accelerating flows.

Eliminating duplication.

That approach has enabled growth.

But it has also created dependence.

When a system removes redundancy, it increases vulnerability.

Because it loses margin for maneuver.

Redundancy appears as the solution.

More routes.

More capacity.

More alternatives.

But the reality is more complex.

Redundancy is not absolute.

It is relative.

Alternatives exist.

But they are not equivalent.

A pipeline can redirect flow.

But it cannot fully replace a maritime chokepoint.

An alternative route can absorb part of the traffic.

But it cannot replicate original efficiency.

Nearshoring can reduce external dependence.

But it does not eliminate global interconnection.

That is the critical point.

Redundancy does not eliminate vulnerability.

It redistributes it.

From a structural perspective, redundancy faces clear limitations.

First, cost.

Building and maintaining alternatives requires investment.

And those investments are not always efficient under normal conditions.

Second, capacity.

Alternative routes have limits.

They cannot absorb full volumes.

Third, time.

Developing new capabilities is not immediate.

Fourth, coordination.

Effective redundancy requires alignment across multiple actors.

This creates a paradox.

The system needs redundancy.

But efficiency tends to eliminate it.

From a strategic perspective, the solution is not to replicate every chokepoint.

That would be unviable.

The solution is to manage dependence.

Reduce concentration.

Increase flexibility.

Build adaptive capacity.

Effective redundancy is not duplication.

It is diversification.

It is distribution.

It is resilience.

Pipelines represent one form of redundancy.

They can divert part of energy flows.

They reduce pressure on certain nodes.

But they do not eliminate maritime dependence.

Alternative routes, such as the Cape of Good Hope, provide continuity.

But at higher cost.

And lower efficiency.

Nearshoring reduces exposure.

It brings production closer to consumption.

But it does not remove the need for global trade.

From an adversarial perspective, redundancy limits impact.

But it does not eliminate it.

Because the system remains interdependent.

And interdependence always creates points of pressure.

The key is not to eliminate vulnerability.

It is to reduce its effect.

Current capabilities show progress.

Greater route diversification.

Investment in infrastructure.

Reconfiguration of supply chains.

But these changes are gradual.

And the system remains exposed.

A proactive doctrine involves several elements.

First, identifying high-concentration points.

Second, developing functional alternatives.

Third, investing in adaptable capacity.

Fourth, coordinating efforts at a global level.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they distribute it.

And by distributing it, they reduce its impact.

Redundancy is not a myth.

But it is not a complete solution.

It is a tool.

A way to manage vulnerability.

Understanding its limits is essential.

Because in a global system, resilience does not come from eliminating dependencies.

It comes from managing them.

And in that management, stability is defined.
