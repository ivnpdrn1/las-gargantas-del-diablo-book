## Chapter 18

### Bosporus and Dardanelles: Europe under pressure

Control of passage in times of conflict

The global system depends on flows.

But it also depends on gateways.

The Bosporus and the Dardanelles are one of them.

A system of straits that connects the Black Sea to the Mediterranean.

A point where transit is not only geographic.

It is strategic.

Because it controls the outlet of a key region.

And conditions its connection to the rest of the world.

This corridor is not the largest in volume.

But it is one of the most sensitive.

Because its control does not depend only on physical conditions.

It depends on decisions.

Regulations.

Political context.

That is the critical point.

Transit through these straits is governed by international agreements.

Rules that define who can pass.

When.

And under what conditions¹.

In times of stability, flow is continuous.

Predictable.

But in periods of tension, that flow can be altered.

Not necessarily through physical blockage.

But through restriction.

Limitation.

Selective control.

The impact of these decisions is not immediate.

But it is significant.

Vessels wait.

Flows slow.

Routes adjust.

And that pressure propagates.

To energy.

To food.

To regional logistics.

The Black Sea depends on this access.

Without it, its connection to the global system is reduced.

That is the mechanism.

Not total interruption.

But control of access.

From a structural perspective, these straits have distinct characteristics.

First, high political sensitivity.

Decisions can change transit conditions.

Second, regional dependence.

Black Sea flows rely on this passage.

Third, physical constraints.

There are no direct alternatives.

Fourth, strategic importance.

Controlling passage means controlling connectivity.

This creates a different kind of vulnerability.

Not based only on geography.

But on governance.

From an adversarial perspective, the objective is not necessarily to close the passage.

It is to influence conditions.

Introduce restrictions.

Generate uncertainty.

That is sufficient to alter the system.

Operators adjust decisions.

Markets react.

Impact begins.

Current capabilities allow monitoring of transit.

But effective protection is complex.

Because it does not depend only on security.

It depends on agreements.

On political stability.

On international coordination.

A proactive doctrine involves several elements.

First, understanding the regulatory framework.

Anticipating possible changes.

Second, monitoring political signals.

Indicators of tension.

Third, contingency planning.

Preparing alternatives.

Fourth, international coordination.

Reducing the impact of unilateral decisions.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they manage it.

The Bosporus and the Dardanelles represent a different dimension of the global system.

Where control is not only physical.

It is political.

And through that control, flow can be altered without direct intervention.

To understand this chokepoint is to understand that vulnerability is not always in infrastructure.

Sometimes it is in the rules that govern it.

And when those rules change, the system changes with them.
