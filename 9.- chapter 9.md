## Chapter 9

### Failures of the current system

What exists… and what does not

The global system works.

But it is not designed to withstand disruption.

It is designed to optimize.

Reduce costs.

Accelerate flows.

Eliminate redundancy.

That design has enabled growth.

But it has also created fragility.

The system’s failures are not mistakes.

They are consequences.

A direct result of how it was built.

The first problem is fragmentation.

Information exists.

But it is not integrated.

Each actor sees only a part.

No one sees the whole.

States.

Companies.

International organizations.

All operate with partial data.

That limits anticipation.

The system reacts.

But it does not always understand.

The second problem is lack of coordination.

Actors are not aligned.

Decisions are made independently.

Sometimes in contradiction.

What benefits one may harm another.

And that lack of coherence reduces effectiveness.

The system does not act as one.

It acts as many.

The third problem is delayed response.

Actions are taken after the event.

When impact has already begun.

When the domino effect is already in motion.

That delay amplifies damage.

The system responds.

But too late.

The fourth problem is the absence of equivalent redundancy.

Alternatives exist.

But they are not equivalent.

They do not replicate capacity.

They do not replicate efficiency.

That limits absorption capacity.

The system can reroute.

But not without cost.

The fifth problem is dependence on trust.

The system is not sustained by infrastructure alone.

It is sustained by expectations.

By perceived stability.

When that confidence erodes, the system changes.

Decisions become more conservative.

Costs increase.

Flow is altered.

The sixth problem is the lack of systemic vision.

Solutions are designed locally.

For specific points.

But the risk is global.

And that disconnect creates gaps.

The point is protected.

But not the network.

These problems do not operate in isolation.

They reinforce each other.

They amplify.

And they create structural vulnerability.

The system does not fail because of a single cause.

It fails through accumulation.

From a strategic perspective, the risk is not only disruption.

It is the inability to respond.

The lack of anticipation.

The fragmentation.

That is the real problem.

From an adversarial perspective, these failures are opportunities.

There is no need to create vulnerability.

It already exists.

It only needs to be identified.

And activated.

Current capabilities allow the system to function.

But not always to protect it.

That is the limit.

An effective doctrine must close these gaps.

First, integration of information.

A complete view.

Second, coordination among actors.

Collective action.

Third, anticipation.

Acting before events occur.

Fourth, development of functional redundancy.

Not equivalent.

But sufficient.

Fifth, strengthening trust.

Reducing uncertainty.

These measures do not eliminate vulnerability.

But they reduce its impact.

The global system is not fragile by accident.

It is fragile by design.

And understanding that design is the first step to correcting it.

Because as long as these failures persist, risk will not disappear.

It will only change form.
