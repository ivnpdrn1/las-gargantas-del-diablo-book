## Chapter 9

### Bab el-Mandeb and Suez: the Europe–Asia artery

The global system does not depend only on points.

It depends on sequences.

And some sequences cannot be interrupted without affecting the whole.

The Bab el-Mandeb–Red Sea–Suez Canal axis is one of them.

It is not a single chokepoint.

It is a continuous corridor.

A system within the system.

And its value lies not only in the volume it carries, but in the function it performs.

It connects Asia with Europe.

It connects production with consumption.

It connects time with cost.

A significant share of global maritime trade moves through this corridor¹.

Containers.

Energy.

Intermediate goods.

Finished products.

Everything flows through this route because it is the most efficient.

The Suez Canal shortens voyages by thousands of kilometers.

It avoids the need to sail around Africa.

It minimizes transit time.

It optimizes cost.

But that efficiency comes at a price.

Dependence.

When a route becomes dominant, the system loses flexibility.

And when it loses flexibility, it increases exposure.

Bab el-Mandeb acts as the entry point.

Suez as the exit.

Between them, a narrow, dense, and continuous corridor.

Not a point.

An artery.

And like any artery, it does not need to be completely blocked to generate impact.

It is enough to reduce its flow.

Or introduce uncertainty.

The vulnerabilities of this corridor are structural.

First, concentration.

High volumes of traffic in a limited space.

Second, operational continuity.

It cannot sustain prolonged interruptions without immediate consequences.

Third, sequence dependency.

Each segment depends on the next.

If one fails, the entire system slows.

Fourth, limited substitution.

The main alternative is to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope.

A viable option, but a costly one.

Longer distances.

Higher fuel consumption.

Greater exposure.

The result is not an immediate collapse.

It is progressive degradation.

Ships wait.

Routes lengthen.

Costs rise.

And that pressure propagates.

Through supply chains.

Through delivery times.

Through prices.

This is the real mechanism.

Not total interruption.

But the accumulation of friction.

From an adversarial perspective, this corridor has a critical characteristic.

It does not need to be closed.

It needs to be questioned.

It is enough to affect the perception of safety.

For the system to react.

Insurance premiums rise.

Operators adjust routes.

Markets price in risk.

Disruption occurs before any actual blockage.

That is the central point.

The system depends as much on confidence as it does on infrastructure.

When that confidence erodes, the impact begins.

Current capabilities allow for monitoring the canal and managing traffic.

But protecting the corridor as a whole is more complex.

It requires multinational coordination.

It requires integrated information.

It requires speed in decision-making.

And it requires anticipation.

A reactive model is insufficient.

By the time a response is activated, the impact is already underway.

A proactive doctrine for this corridor involves several elements.

Full visibility across the entire axis.

Not only the canal.

Early identification of pressure signals.

Changes in routes.

Rising costs.

Variations in transit times.

Coordination mechanisms that enable rapid response.

Strategies that reduce flow concentration.

None of these measures eliminate risk.

But all reduce its ability to escalate.

The Bab el-Mandeb–Suez axis is not just a route.

It is a central component of the global system.

A point where multiple dependencies converge.

And where even limited disruption can generate amplified effects.

To understand this corridor is to understand how efficiency becomes vulnerability.

And how that vulnerability can be managed before it becomes crisis.

Because in an interdependent system, not everything needs to fail.

Only enough.

And in this corridor, enough can be very little.
