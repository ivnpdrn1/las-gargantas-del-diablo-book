## Chapter 13

### Malacca: Asia’s chokepoint

Strategic dependence of major economies

The global system depends on energy.

But it also depends on routes.

And in Asia, one of those routes is decisive.

The Strait of Malacca.

One of the busiest maritime corridors in the world¹.

A narrow passage.

With global impact.

Malacca connects the Indian Ocean to the Pacific.

It connects the Middle East to Asia.

It connects supply with demand.

But above all, it connects energy with industry.

Countries such as China, Japan, and South Korea rely on this passage to secure their energy supply.

Oil.

Gas.

Essential goods.

It is not an option.

It is a necessity.

The volume that flows through Malacca is not only high.

It is critical.

And that criticality is not defined only by quantity.

It is defined by dependence.

Unlike other chokepoints, where partial alternatives exist, Malacca concentrates a significant share of Asia’s flow in a single point.

That creates exposure.

If flow is disrupted, impact does not disperse.

It concentrates.

The vulnerabilities of Malacca are structural.

First, traffic density.

Large vessels transit through a confined space.

This increases sensitivity to any incident.

Second, limited maneuverability.

Geographic conditions restrict rapid rerouting.

Third, proximity to multiple jurisdictions.

Coordination is not always immediate.

Fourth, energy dependence.

A substantial portion of Asia’s supply depends on this point.

The impact of disruption does not appear as immediate collapse.

It appears as pressure.

Flow continues.

But with friction.

Transit times extend.

Costs increase.

Decisions shift.

And that pressure propagates.

To production.

To prices.

To economic stability.

From a strategic perspective, Malacca is not just a chokepoint.

It is a multiplier.

Any disruption is amplified by the scale of the system that depends on it.

From an adversarial perspective, this makes it a high-value target.

Not because of its physical fragility.

But because of its systemic sensitivity.

It is not necessary to close the strait.

It is enough to introduce uncertainty.

An incident.

A threat.

A signal of risk.

That is sufficient to trigger reactions.

Insurers raise premiums.

Operators adjust routes.

Markets react.

Impact begins before full disruption.

That is the mechanism.

The system does not wait for confirmation.

It anticipates.

Current capabilities allow monitoring of transit.

But effective protection requires more than observation.

It requires anticipation.

It requires regional coordination.

It requires rapid response capability.

A proactive doctrine involves several elements.

First, full visibility of flow.

Second, early identification of signals.

Changes in navigation.

Increases in transit time.

Variations in cost.

Third, coordination among actors.

Collective action.

Fourth, development of alternatives.

Reducing concentration.

These measures do not eliminate vulnerability.

But they reduce its impact.

Malacca represents Asia’s strategic dependence.

And, by extension, a key vulnerability in the global system.

Because when a single point concentrates so much flow, it does not need to fail completely.

It only needs to stop functioning as expected.

And within that deviation, however small, lies the risk.
