## Chapter 13

### The Cape of Good Hope: the emergency route

The global system is designed for efficiency.

Not for redundancy.

And when that efficiency fails, the system looks for alternatives.

The Cape of Good Hope is one of them.

It is not a chokepoint in the traditional sense.

It does not concentrate flow.

It does not restrict it.

It does not channel it.

But it becomes critical when other points stop functioning.

It is the global system’s emergency route.

The path that activates when primary routes degrade.

When the Suez Canal loses capacity.

When the Red Sea becomes unstable.

When continuity of flow is compromised.

In those moments, the system reconfigures.

And the Cape shifts from a secondary option to a dominant route.

But that transition comes at a cost.

The Cape of Good Hope implies significantly longer distances.

More days at sea.

Higher fuel consumption.

Greater exposure to risk.

The impact is not immediate.

But it is cumulative.

Each additional day in transit increases costs.

Reduces efficiency.

Alters delivery schedules.

And that effect propagates.

Through supply chains.

Through prices.

Through logistical planning.

From a structural perspective, the Cape represents the absence of true alternatives.

Because while it exists as a route, it does not replicate the efficiency of primary chokepoints.

It is not equivalent.

It is an imperfect substitute.

That is the critical point.

The global system is not built with equivalent redundancies.

It is built with detours.

And a detour is not a solution.

It is an adaptation.

When multiple actors simultaneously shift to this route, the system encounters a new form of pressure.

More congestion.

Greater competition for resources.

Higher operational costs.

Flow continues.

But under less efficient conditions.

This change does not produce an immediate collapse.

It produces a transformation.

A slower system.

A more expensive system.

A more uncertain system.

From an adversarial perspective, the Cape is not a target.

It is a consequence.

There is no need to disrupt this route directly.

It is enough to affect primary chokepoints.

The system will redirect itself toward the Cape.

And in that redirection, it absorbs the impact.

That is the mechanism.

Flow is not interrupted.

It is forced to change.

And that change generates pressure.

Current capabilities are not designed to optimize this route.

They are designed to avoid relying on it.

That creates a gap.

When the system depends on the Cape, it operates outside its optimal state.

And in that state, it is more vulnerable.

Effective protection does not consist of securing the Cape itself.

It consists of preventing the system from having to depend on it for sustained periods.

However, ignoring this route would be a mistake.

Because in disruption scenarios, it becomes the primary channel of continuity.

For that reason, a proactive strategy must consider several elements.

First, absorption capacity.

Assess how much flow this route can handle without generating operational breakdown.

Second, logistical preparation.

Adjust supply chains for prolonged rerouting scenarios.

Third, cost management.

Anticipate the economic impact of increased time and distance.

Fourth, global coordination.

Flow reconfiguration is not local.

It is systemic.

These measures do not eliminate impact.

But they reduce its intensity.

The Cape of Good Hope represents an uncomfortable truth about the global system.

Resilience is not guaranteed.

It is limited.

And when those limits are reached, the system does not collapse.

It transforms.

Understanding that transformation is essential.

Because not all crises appear as disruptions.

Some appear as degradation.

And within that degradation, silent but persistent, lies the real cost.
