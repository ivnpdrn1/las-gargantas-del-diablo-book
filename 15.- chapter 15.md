## Chapter 15

### Bosporus and Dardanelles: Europe under pressure

Not all chokepoints connect oceans.

Some connect systems.

The Bosporus and the Dardanelles form one of those points.

A corridor linking the Black Sea to the Mediterranean.

A mandatory passage.

A point where flow cannot be bypassed.

This system does not only connect geographies.

It connects dynamics.

From the Black Sea, energy flows.

Grains flow.

Raw materials flow.

Everything leaving that region for the global system passes through here.

There are no equivalent routes.

No functional alternatives.

That is the critical point.

Dependence is absolute.

Unlike other chokepoints, where risk lies in disruption of transit, here risk lies in control of access.

Flow does not stop only because of physical conditions.

It can be altered by decisions.

By regulation.

By restrictions.

This introduces a different dimension.

The chokepoint is not only geographic.

It is political.

The vulnerabilities of this corridor are specific.

First, concentration of access.

A single passage for multiple critical flows.

Second, dependence on a regulatory framework.

Transit is not automatic.

It is governed by rules.

Third, geopolitical sensitivity.

The region is embedded in power dynamics.

Fourth, connection to other chokepoints.

What happens in the Black Sea is amplified here.

The impact of disruption is not immediate as in other cases.

But it is cumulative.

Flow continues.

But under changing conditions.

Restrictions.

Delays.

Uncertainty.

And that variability generates pressure.

On markets.

On delivery times.

On regional stability.

From a strategic perspective, this corridor does not need to be closed.

It only needs to be modified.

Conditions can change.

Access can be adjusted.

Friction can be introduced.

The system responds.

Routes adapt.

Costs increase.

Decisions shift.

That is the mechanism.

Not interruption.

But control.

From an adversarial perspective, this expands the range of options.

No physical action is required.

What is required is influence.

The ability to alter conditions.

To affect perceptions.

To reshape expectations.

That is sufficient to generate impact.

Current capabilities allow for managing transit.

But effective protection requires more than operation.

It requires institutional stability.

It requires predictability.

It requires coordination.

A proactive doctrine in this context involves several elements.

First, clarity of transit rules.

Reducing uncertainty.

Second, coordination mechanisms among actors.

Avoiding fragmented decisions.

Third, monitoring of pressure signals.

Changes in volume.

Variations in transit time.

Shifts in operating conditions.

Fourth, diversification of routes where possible.

Reducing absolute dependence.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they contain it.

The Bosporus and the Dardanelles represent a different dimension of chokepoints.

Where geography intersects with policy.

Where flow depends as much on physical conditions as on decisions.

To understand this corridor is to understand that control over the system is not always exercised through disruption.

Sometimes it is exercised through regulation.

And within that regulation, even in its smallest adjustments, lies power.

Because in an interdependent system, it is not necessary to stop the flow to generate impact.

It is enough to change its conditions.

And within that change, often invisible, lies the pressure.
