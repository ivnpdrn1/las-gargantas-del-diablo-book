## Chapter 15

### Panama: when climate stops the economy

Infrastructure, water, and non-military vulnerability

Not all chokepoints are defined by geopolitics.

Some are defined by nature.

The Panama Canal is one of them.

A critical node in the global system.

Designed to shorten distances.

Optimize routes.

Accelerate trade.

For decades, it has been a symbol of efficiency.

A shortcut between oceans.

A structural solution to a geographic constraint.

But that solution depends on a fundamental resource.

Water.

The Panama Canal is not a passive passage.

It is an active system.

It operates through locks.

And those locks require large volumes of freshwater.

Each transit consumes a limited resource.

That is the critical point.

Unlike other chokepoints, where vulnerability comes from actors or geopolitical tensions, here it comes from environmental conditions.

Droughts.

Shifts in rainfall patterns.

Climate variability.

When water levels drop, canal capacity declines.

Fewer transits.

Restrictions on vessel size.

Longer waiting times.

Flow does not stop.

But it degrades.

And that degradation generates impact.

The Panama Canal may not carry the same volume as other chokepoints.

But it concentrates efficiency.

It is an optimized route.

When that optimization is lost, the system adjusts.

Routes lengthen.

Costs increase.

Transit times extend.

The impact is not immediate.

But it is cumulative.

And sustained.

From a systemic perspective, Panama introduces a different type of vulnerability.

It is not intentional.

It is structural.

It does not depend on immediate human decisions.

It depends on evolving conditions that cannot be fully controlled.

This changes the logic of protection.

It is not about deterrence.

It is not about presence.

It is about management.

Management of water resources.

Management of capacity.

Management of expectations.

From a strategic perspective, the risk is not total closure.

It is progressive loss of efficiency.

Reduced capacity.

Greater uncertainty.

Increased pressure.

This type of disruption does not generate immediate headlines.

But it produces persistent effects.

Supply chains adjust gradually.

Costs rise steadily.

Decisions adapt.

The system does not collapse.

But it becomes less efficient.

And in an optimized system, loss of efficiency is vulnerability.

Alternatives exist.

Longer routes.

Other canals.

Logistical adjustments.

But none offer the same balance of time and cost.

When Panama loses capacity, the global system pays the difference.

Effective protection of this chokepoint requires a different approach.

First, water management.

Without water, there is no canal.

Second, infrastructure adaptation.

Adjusting capacity to changing conditions.

Third, forward planning.

Anticipating reductions before they occur.

Fourth, route diversification.

Reducing absolute dependence.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they reduce it.

Panama represents a key reality of the global system.

Not all disruptions come from conflict.

Some come from natural limits.

And those limits cannot be ignored.

Because in a system where efficiency is central, any sustained constraint becomes structural pressure.

The Panama Canal does not need to close to affect the global economy.

It only needs to operate below capacity.

And within that reduced margin, even if small, lies the impact.
