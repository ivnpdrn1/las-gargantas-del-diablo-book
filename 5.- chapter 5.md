## Chapter 5

### How a Chokepoint Is Protected

A Doctrine of Proactive Defense

Protecting a chokepoint is not about defending a place.

It is about ensuring that flow continues.

That is the fundamental difference between a reactive approach and an effective doctrine. For years, the protection of critical points has been understood primarily as a matter of presence: monitor, patrol, respond. In an interdependent global system, however, that approach is insufficient.

A chokepoint does not fail only when it is blocked. It fails when its operation becomes uncertain. Confidence in its continuity is as important as its physical capacity to function. Protection must therefore focus not only on preventing disruptions, but on sustaining stable flow under pressure.

A proactive doctrine begins with a simple principle: the objective is not to prevent every event, but to ensure that no event escalates into systemic disruption.

Achieving this requires structuring protection in complementary layers that operate simultaneously.

The first layer is domain awareness. What cannot be seen cannot be protected. This involves integrating information from multiple sources: maritime traffic, operational conditions, economic signals, and open data. The challenge is not the volume of information, but its integration into a coherent picture that reflects what is happening in real time.

The second layer is early detection. Critical systems rarely fail without warning. Shifts in traffic patterns, increases in logistical costs, variations in transit times, or changes in market behavior are indicators of mounting pressure. Identifying these signals before they escalate reduces reaction time and helps prevent instability¹.

The third layer is credible deterrence. Protection depends not only on the ability to respond, but on how that ability is perceived. When the cost of disrupting a chokepoint is visible and consistent, incentives to do so decrease. Deterrence does not require constant confrontation, but it does require clarity and predictability in response.

The fourth layer is operational coordination. In an interdependent environment, no single actor controls the system. Effective response depends on coordination across multiple entities, both public and private. Without clear mechanisms for decision and execution, even strong technical capabilities lose effectiveness. Speed of response becomes a decisive factor.

The fifth layer is systemic redundancy. No chokepoint should function as a single point of failure. Alternative routes, strategic storage, and logistical flexibility allow the system to absorb disruptions without disproportionate effects. Redundancy does not eliminate risk, but it reduces its impact².

These layers do not operate independently. They form a system. Weakness in any one of them compromises the effectiveness of the whole.

A proactive doctrine also requires a shift in timing. The traditional model reacts after an event occurs. In the context of chokepoints, that is insufficient. The impact begins before the response is fully deployed. It is therefore necessary to define action thresholds in advance, establish clear protocols, and conduct simulations that anticipate potential scenarios.

Measurement is another essential element. Protection cannot rely on perception alone. It must be assessed through concrete indicators: detection time, decision time, flow continuity, and recovery capacity. These metrics allow for continuous adjustment and improvement of the strategy³.

Despite their importance, significant gaps remain. Information exists, but it is not always integrated. Capabilities are available, but not necessarily coordinated. Decisions often depend on multiple actors, introducing delays at critical moments. And redundancy, in many systems, has been reduced in favor of efficiency.

The result is a system that can observe what is happening, but not always react with the speed required to contain its impact.

Effective protection of a chokepoint is not achieved by eliminating risk. It is achieved by managing it in advance. This means accepting that disruptions will occur, but designing the system so they cannot escalate.

Ultimately, the difference between vulnerability and resilience does not lie in the absence of threats, but in the ability to maintain function under pressure.

A well-protected chokepoint is not one where nothing ever happens.

It is one where, even when something happens, the flow continues.
