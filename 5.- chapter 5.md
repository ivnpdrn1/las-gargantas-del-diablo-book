## Chapter 5

### Adversarial thinking

How someone seeking to disrupt the global system would think

The global system must not only be understood.

It must be anticipated.

And to anticipate it, it must be viewed from a different perspective.

Not from those who use it.

But from those who seek to disrupt it.

Adversarial thinking does not focus on the system’s complexity.

It focuses on its vulnerability.

It does not aim to understand everything.

It aims to identify the critical point.

The place where a limited action can generate a broad impact.

That is the principle.

Maximize effect.

Minimize effort.

From this logic, the global system reveals clear characteristics.

High interdependence.

Concentrated flows.

Extreme optimization.

Dependence on trust.

Each of these characteristics is an opportunity.

Not to destroy the system.

But to alter it.

The objective is not necessarily total collapse.

It is disruption.

Introducing uncertainty.

Increasing costs.

Reducing efficiency.

That is enough.

The first element of adversarial thinking is target selection.

Not all points are equal.

Priority is given to those where impact amplifies.

Energy chokepoints.

Critical logistical corridors.

High-concentration nodes.

The criterion is not visibility.

It is effect.

The second element is the method of intervention.

Direct action is not always required.

A physical event is one option.

But not the only one.

One can act on perception.

On conditions.

On the systems that enable flow.

Disruption can be indirect.

And still effective.

The third element is timing.

Not all moments generate the same impact.

The system has phases.

Demand peaks.

Periods of high sensitivity.

Acting at those points maximizes effect.

The fourth element is scale.

A large-scale event is not necessary.

A small event, in the right place, at the right time, can generate amplified effects.

That is the use of the system against itself.

The fifth element is propagation.

The initial action is not the final objective.

It is the trigger.

The system does the rest.

Chain reactions.

Market adjustments.

Operational changes.

Impact amplifies.

That is the true outcome.

From this perspective, the global system is not only a network of flows.

It is a network of vulnerabilities.

And those vulnerabilities are not hidden.

They are embedded in its design.

Efficiency reduces redundancy.

Concentration reduces alternatives.

Interdependence increases propagation.

Adversarial thinking identifies these elements.

And uses them.

Current capabilities are designed to respond to visible events.

But not always to anticipate indirect actions.

That is the challenge.

Disruption will not always be obvious.

It may appear as gradual change.

As accumulated pressure.

As altered conditions.

An effective protection doctrine must incorporate this perspective.

Not to replicate it.

But to anticipate it.

First, identify points of high sensitivity.

Second, analyze possible forms of intervention.

Third, monitor early signals.

Fourth, act before impact amplifies.

These actions do not eliminate risk.

But they reduce its capacity to propagate.

Adversarial thinking does not necessarily aim to destroy the system.

It aims to exploit it.

To use its own structure to generate impact.

Understanding this is essential.

Because in an interdependent system, the greatest vulnerability is not what can be attacked.

It is what can be activated.

And within that activation, often minimal, lies the risk.
