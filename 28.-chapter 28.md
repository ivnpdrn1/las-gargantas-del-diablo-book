## Chapter 28

### The impossible balance

Efficiency vs. resilience

The global system faces a permanent tension.

Efficiency.

Or resilience.

Not both at their maximum.

That is the dilemma.

For decades, efficiency was the priority.

Reducing costs.

Optimizing flows.

Eliminating redundancy.

Accelerating decisions.

That model enabled growth.

Scale.

Global integration.

But it came at a cost.

Fragility.

Resilience follows a different logic.

More redundancy.

More idle capacity.

More flexibility.

More margin for error.

But it also implies cost.

Higher investment.

Lower immediate efficiency.

That is the balance.

It is not technical.

It is structural.

Both extremes cannot be maximized at the same time.

Every decision in favor of efficiency reduces resilience.

Every decision in favor of resilience reduces efficiency.

The current system leans toward efficiency.

Because its benefits are visible.

Immediate.

Measurable.

The costs of fragility are deferred.

They materialize in crises.

In disruptions.

In moments of stress.

That time gap shapes decisions.

Optimization happens today.

Risk is assumed tomorrow.

From a structural perspective, this balance defines system behavior.

Under normal conditions, efficiency dominates.

The system flows.

Operates with precision.

Minimizes cost.

But under stress, that efficiency becomes a constraint.

There is no margin.

No absorption capacity.

The system degrades.

Resilience, by contrast, reveals itself in crisis.

It allows continuity.

Reduces impact.

But its cost is permanent.

Even when there is no disruption.

That is the challenge.

Investing in capacity that may not always be used.

From a strategic perspective, the solution is not choosing one extreme.

It is managing the balance.

Determining where efficiency is required.

And where resilience is essential.

Not all systems require the same level.

Not all chokepoints carry the same criticality.

That is the key point.

Allocation.

Optimizing without eliminating margin.

Protecting without paralyzing the system.

From an adversarial perspective, this balance is exploitable.

A highly efficient system is more vulnerable.

A highly resilient system is more expensive.

The objective is not destruction.

It is pressure.

Pushing the system to its limits.

Forcing decisions.

Increasing costs.

Reducing efficiency.

That is the mechanism.

Current capabilities show attempts to adjust.

Route diversification.

Supply chain reconfiguration.

Infrastructure investment.

But the balance remains unstable.

Decisions still favor efficiency.

Resilience is activated afterward.

Not beforehand.

An effective doctrine requires several elements.

First, identifying critical levels.

Where resilience is indispensable.

Second, defining thresholds.

How much risk is acceptable.

Third, investing selectively.

Not everywhere.

But where it matters most.

Fourth, measuring impact.

Not only immediate costs.

But systemic consequences.

These measures do not eliminate the tension.

But they manage it.

The balance between efficiency and resilience is not fixed.

It is dynamic.

It evolves with context.

With environment.

With risk.

Understanding this is essential.

Because the global system will not fail due to lack of efficiency.

It will fail due to lack of resilience.

And within that difference, its future is defined.
