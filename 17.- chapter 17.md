## Chapter 17

### The Black Sea: the invisible chokepoint

Grains, fertilizers, and global stability

The global system does not depend only on energy and manufacturing.

It depends on food.

And in that domain, there is a chokepoint that is rarely perceived as such.

The Black Sea.

Not because of its geography.

But because of its function.

A significant share of global grain and fertilizer trade passes through this region¹.

Wheat.

Corn.

Oils.

Critical inputs for agricultural production.

This is not a flow as visible as oil.

It does not trigger immediate reactions in financial markets.

But its impact is profound.

And in many cases, more sensitive.

Because it affects the foundation.

Food.

Social stability.

The Black Sea connects production zones with global markets.

Exporting regions with dependent ones.

Particularly across Africa, the Middle East, and parts of Asia.

That is the critical point.

This is not only trade.

It is food security.

When this flow is disrupted, the impact is not immediate across all systems.

But it is progressive.

And cumulative.

Food prices rise.

Agricultural inputs become more expensive.

Future production is affected.

Pressure propagates.

Not only through markets.

But through societies.

From a structural perspective, this chokepoint has distinct characteristics.

First, high concentration of exports.

A few actors with large shares.

Second, regional dependence.

Multiple countries rely on this flow.

Third, low initial visibility.

Impact is not immediate.

Fourth, high social sensitivity.

Food price increases generate instability.

This creates a different type of risk.

Not only economic.

But social and political.

From an adversarial perspective, the Black Sea does not need to be fully closed.

It is enough to alter flow.

Reduce exports.

Increase uncertainty.

That alone is sufficient to generate impact.

Markets react.

Prices rise.

Decisions change.

Impact amplifies over time.

That is the mechanism.

Not total interruption.

But sustained pressure.

Current capabilities allow monitoring of exports and transit.

But effective protection is complex.

Because it is not only about routes.

It is about production.

Agreements.

Regional stability.

A proactive doctrine involves several elements.

First, diversification of sources.

Reducing dependence.

Second, strategic reserves.

Absorbing shocks.

Third, international coordination.

Ensuring continuity.

Fourth, monitoring of signals.

Changes in production.

Variations in exports.

Indicators of pressure.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they reduce it.

The Black Sea represents an invisible chokepoint.

Not because it does not exist.

But because its impact is not immediate.

Yet when it manifests, it is deep.

And difficult to contain.

To understand this point is to understand that the global system depends not only on visible flows.

It depends on essential flows.

And when those flows are disrupted, the impact is not only economic.

It is human.

And at that level, disruption takes on a different dimension.
