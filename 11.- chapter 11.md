## Chapter 11

### The Taiwan Strait: the chokepoint of the 21st century

The global system no longer depends only on energy.

It depends on technology.

And in that transition, a new chokepoint has emerged.

The Taiwan Strait.

It is not the narrowest passage.

It is not the most heavily trafficked.

But it is, perhaps, the most critical in terms of systemic impact.

Because at this point, it is not only trade that flows.

Industrial capacity flows.

Innovation flows.

Economic power flows.

The Taiwan Strait serves as a central link between one of the most dynamic regions in the world and the global system.

A significant share of international trade moves through its waters¹.

But its importance goes beyond volume.

It sits at the core of the most important technological supply chain of the 21st century.

Semiconductors.

Electronic components.

Systems that support everything from automotive production to artificial intelligence.

The production of these elements is highly concentrated.

And a substantial portion of that concentration is located in Taiwan.

That transforms the strait into more than a route.

It becomes a point of critical dependence.

Unlike other chokepoints, where impact is measured in energy or goods, here it is measured in productive capacity.

Without semiconductors, the modern economy loses continuity.

Factories slow.

Technological systems are delayed.

Innovation decelerates.

The impact is not immediate in the way oil disruptions are.

But it is deep.

And prolonged.

The vulnerabilities of this chokepoint are distinct.

First, industrial concentration.

A significant share of global production depends on a limited geography.

Second, geopolitical tension.

The region sits within one of the most sensitive strategic environments in the world.

Third, technological dependence.

There are no immediate substitutes at the required scale.

Fourth, supply chain complexity.

This is not a single product.

It is a multi-stage, interdependent system.

This creates a different kind of risk.

Not based solely on disruption of transit.

But on disruption of capacity.

From an adversarial perspective, this changes the logic.

It is not necessary to fully block the strait.

It is enough to destabilize the region.

To introduce uncertainty.

To affect confidence in the continuity of production.

That alone is sufficient to trigger reactions.

Companies adjust inventories.

Governments reconsider industrial policy.

Markets respond.

Impact begins before any physical disruption occurs.

This is the new paradigm.

Disruption is not only logistical.

It is structural.

The system does not respond only to interruption of flow.

It responds to the possibility of losing capacity.

Current capabilities allow for monitoring transit and analyzing production.

But effective protection of this chokepoint requires a different approach.

It is not only about securing passage.

It is about securing the continuity of production.

A proactive doctrine in this context involves several elements.

First, diversification.

Reducing concentration of production.

Second, technological redundancy.

Developing alternative capabilities.

Third, international coordination.

Dependence is global.

The response must be as well.

Fourth, anticipation.

Identifying risk signals before they materialize.

These measures do not eliminate vulnerability.

But they reduce its impact.

The Taiwan Strait represents a shift in the nature of chokepoints.

From physical to systemic.

From logistical to industrial.

Understanding this point is understanding where global risk is heading.

Because in the 21st century, it is not enough for energy to flow.

Technology must flow as well.

And when that technology is concentrated in a single location, the system becomes vulnerable.

Not to immediate interruption.

But to loss of continuity.

And within that loss, even if partial, lies the greatest risk.
