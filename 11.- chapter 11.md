## Chapter 11

### Hormuz: the switch of global oil

The most sensitive point in the global energy system

The global energy system has many nodes.

But few are decisive.

The Strait of Hormuz is one of them.

Not because of its size.

But because of its function.

A substantial share of the world’s oil passes through this passage¹.

That flow is not replaceable in the short term.

There is no alternative with equivalent capacity and efficiency.

That makes Hormuz a switch.

It does not control all energy.

But it controls continuity.

That is the critical point.

Oil is not just a resource.

It is the operational foundation of the global system.

Transport.

Industry.

Production.

Costs.

Everything depends on its flow.

When that flow is disrupted, the impact propagates.

Rapidly.

The first level of impact is the market.

Oil prices react immediately.

They do not wait for confirmation.

They respond to signals.

To risk.

To perception.

That movement is instantaneous.

The second level is economic.

The price increase spreads.

To fuels.

To transportation.

To production costs.

Inflation accelerates.

The impact distributes across the system.

The third level is operational.

Supply chains are affected.

Not only by cost.

But by uncertainty.

Decisions change.

Routes adjust.

The system loses efficiency.

The fourth level is financial.

Markets become more volatile.

Risk perception increases.

Investment slows.

Decisions are delayed.

The fifth level is geopolitical.

Energy becomes an instrument of pressure.

Relationships shift.

Policy decisions accelerate.

The system enters a state of tension.

These levels are not sequential.

They occur simultaneously.

They reinforce each other.

That is the true impact.

Hormuz is not just a chokepoint.

It is a systemic multiplier.

The vulnerabilities of this point are clear.

First, concentration of flow.

High volumes in a limited space.

Second, global dependence.

Multiple economies rely on this passage.

Third, limited substitution capacity.

Alternatives cannot absorb the same volume.

Fourth, energy market sensitivity.

Small disruptions produce large effects.

From an adversarial perspective, Hormuz does not need to be fully closed.

It is enough to introduce uncertainty.

Increase perceived risk.

Alter conditions.

That alone is sufficient to activate the system.

Prices react.

Markets adjust.

Impact begins.

That is the mechanism.

Not total interruption.

But pressure.

Current capabilities allow monitoring of transit.

And response to events.

But effective protection requires anticipation.

Not only reaction.

A proactive doctrine for Hormuz involves several elements.

First, continuous monitoring of signals.

Changes in regional activity.

Rising tensions.

Second, rapid response mechanisms.

Acting before impact amplifies.

Third, development of alternatives.

Reducing concentration of flow.

Fourth, international coordination.

Dependence is global.

The response must be as well.

These measures do not eliminate vulnerability.

But they reduce its impact.

Hormuz represents the fragility of the global energy system.

A point where flow is critical.

And where any disruption can generate amplified effects.

To understand Hormuz is to understand how energy defines the system.

And how its disruption, even partial, can redefine the global balance.
