## Chapter 12

### Bab el-Mandeb and Suez: the Europe–Asia artery

The Red Sea as a zone of constant pressure

The global system does not depend only on points.

It depends on corridors.

Continuous sequences where flow cannot be interrupted without consequences.

The Bab el-Mandeb–Red Sea–Suez Canal axis is one of those corridors.

It is not a single chokepoint.

It is an artery.

A system within the system.

And its value lies not only in volume.

It lies in function.

It connects Asia with Europe.

It connects production with consumption.

It connects time with cost.

A significant share of global trade moves through this corridor¹.

Containers.

Energy.

Intermediate goods.

Finished products.

Everything flows through this route because it is the most efficient.

The Suez Canal shortens voyages by thousands of kilometers.

It avoids sailing around Africa.

It minimizes transit time.

It optimizes cost.

But that efficiency creates dependence.

And dependence creates vulnerability.

When a route becomes dominant, the system loses flexibility.

Bab el-Mandeb is the entry.

Suez is the exit.

Between them, a narrow, dense, continuous corridor.

Not a point.

A concentrated flow.

And that flow is sensitive.

It does not need to stop completely to generate impact.

It only needs to degrade.

To slow.

To become uncertain.

The vulnerabilities of this corridor are structural.

First, concentration.

High volumes in a limited space.

Second, continuity.

It cannot sustain prolonged interruptions without immediate effects.

Third, sequential dependence.

Each segment depends on the next.

If one fails, the system slows.

Fourth, limited substitution.

The main alternative is rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope.

More distance.

More time.

More cost.

The impact is not instantaneous as in energy markets.

It is progressive.

But persistent.

Ships wait.

Routes lengthen.

Costs increase.

And that pressure propagates.

Through supply chains.

Through prices.

Through logistical planning.

From an adversarial perspective, this corridor has a key characteristic.

It does not need to be closed.

It only needs to become risky.

To affect the perception of security.

To introduce uncertainty.

That is sufficient to alter behavior.

Insurers adjust premiums.

Operators change routes.

Markets react.

Impact begins before total disruption.

That is the mechanism.

The system depends as much on confidence as it does on infrastructure.

When that confidence erodes, flow is altered.

Current capabilities allow monitoring of traffic.

But protecting the entire corridor is more complex.

It requires coordination.

It requires integration.

It requires speed.

And it requires anticipation.

A reactive model is insufficient.

By the time response arrives, the impact is already underway.

A proactive doctrine for this corridor involves several elements.

First, full visibility of the axis.

Not only the canal.

Second, early identification of signals.

Changes in traffic.

Rising costs.

Variations in transit time.

Third, coordination among actors.

Rapid response.

Fourth, strategies to reduce concentration.

Diversifying flow.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they reduce its capacity to amplify.

The Bab el-Mandeb–Suez axis is not just a route.

It is a central component of the global system.

A point where multiple dependencies converge.

And where even limited disruption can generate global effects.

To understand this corridor is to understand how efficiency becomes vulnerability.

And how that vulnerability can be managed before it becomes crisis.

Because in an interdependent system, not everything needs to fail.

Only enough.

And in this corridor, enough can be very little.
