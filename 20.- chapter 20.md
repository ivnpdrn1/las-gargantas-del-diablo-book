## Chapter 20

### If the Red Sea becomes impassable

Total reconfiguration of global trade

The global system depends on efficient routes.

Not infinite ones.

The Red Sea is one of those routes.

A corridor that connects Asia to Europe through the Suez Canal.

An axis where time and cost are optimized.

But that optimization depends on one condition.

Continuity.

If the Red Sea becomes impassable, the system does not stop.

It reconfigures.

And that reconfiguration has consequences.

The first effect is logistical.

Vessels can no longer transit the corridor.

They are forced to reroute.

The Cape of Good Hope becomes the alternative.

Distances increase.

Thousands of additional kilometers.

Extra days at sea.

Flow continues.

But under new conditions.

Slower.

More expensive.

The second effect is economic.

Longer transit times translate into higher costs.

More fuel.

Greater resource use.

Higher freight rates.

That cost propagates.

Through supply chains.

Through final prices.

Through inflation.

The third effect is operational.

Supply chains lose precision.

Transit times become less predictable.

Inventories must be adjusted.

Planning becomes more complex.

The system does not collapse.

But it loses efficiency.

The fourth effect is systemic.

Not all actors adjust in the same way.

Some have adaptive capacity.

Others do not.

This creates asymmetries.

Advantages for some.

Pressure for others.

The balance of the system shifts.

The fifth effect is strategic.

The Red Sea is not just a route.

It is a multiplier of efficiency.

When it ceases to function, the system reveals its dependence.

And that dependence becomes vulnerability.

From an adversarial perspective, this scenario does not require a total closure.

It is enough to create conditions of risk.

Uncertainty.

Instability.

That alone is sufficient to alter system behavior.

Operators avoid the route.

Insurers adjust coverage.

Markets react.

Impact begins before full disruption.

That is the mechanism.

Not blockade.

But deterrence of use.

Current capabilities allow for monitoring the region.

But effective protection requires more than observation.

It requires anticipation.

It requires international coordination.

It requires rapid response capacity.

A proactive doctrine involves several elements.

First, early identification of signals.

Changes in security conditions.

Variations in traffic.

Rising costs.

Second, logistical preparation.

Planning for prolonged rerouting.

Third, management of economic impact.

Anticipating effects on prices and supply chains.

Fourth, route diversification.

Reducing dependence on a single corridor.

These measures do not eliminate impact.

But they contain it.

If the Red Sea becomes impassable, the global system does not collapse.

But it changes significantly.

It becomes slower.

More expensive.

More uncertain.

And within that change, the balance is redefined.

Because in an optimized system, any disruption to efficiency produces amplified effects.

The Red Sea does not need to be fully closed to generate global impact.

It only needs to stop being reliable.

And in that loss of reliability lies disruption.
