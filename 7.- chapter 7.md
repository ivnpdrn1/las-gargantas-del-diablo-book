## Chapter 7

### Proactivity vs Reaction

Why arriving late is the greatest strategic risk

The global system does not fail only because of what happens.

It fails because of when it responds.

For decades, risk management in critical infrastructure has been built on an implicit assumption: events are exceptions that must be managed once they occur. That approach worked in environments where impacts were contained and response times allowed for damage control.

Today, that logic is no longer sufficient.

In an interdependent system, the difference between anticipating and reacting is not operational.

It is structural.

When a disruption occurs at a chokepoint, the impact does not wait for a response to be activated. It begins the moment the system perceives risk. Markets adjust prices, routes are reconfigured, and logistical decisions accelerate before any full interruption takes place.

In that context, reacting means arriving late.

Proactivity is not about preventing every event. It is about acting before those events generate systemic consequences. It requires identifying weak signals, defining action thresholds, and activating response mechanisms before the system enters a phase of degradation.

The first element of a proactive strategy is anticipation. Chokepoints do not fail without warning. Shifts in traffic patterns, increases in waiting times, changes in insurance costs, or alterations in regional activity are signals that precede disruption. Ignoring them does not eliminate risk; it shifts it into a phase where response will be more costly and less effective¹.

The second element is the definition of thresholds. Not every signal requires immediate action, but every signal must be tied to clear decision criteria. Without thresholds, information becomes noise. With thresholds, information becomes action.

The third element is speed of decision. In highly connected systems, time is not linear. A delay of hours can amplify into days of impact. The ability to decide quickly is not only a matter of technology. It depends on governance structures that allow action without unnecessary friction.

The fourth element is coordinated execution. Proactivity loses effectiveness if actions are not aligned. Protecting chokepoints involves multiple actors. Without predefined coordination mechanisms, each individual response may be correct, but the collective result may still be insufficient.

The fifth element is adaptability. Proactivity is not a one-time action. It is a continuous process. As the environment evolves, signals change and thresholds must be adjusted. A system that does not adapt loses its capacity to anticipate.

Despite its importance, the dominant model remains reactive. Resources are mobilized after the event occurs. Disruption is addressed rather than managed before it escalates. This approach creates a false sense of control.

The system appears to function.

Until it no longer does.

The difference between a resilient system and a vulnerable one does not lie in the absence of events, but in how those events are managed in their early stages. A reactive system absorbs shocks. A proactive system limits them.

In the context of chokepoints, this distinction is critical. The concentration of flows reduces margins for error. The synchronization of dependencies amplifies effects. And the speed of propagation eliminates the possibility of delayed responses.

Proactivity also redefines resource allocation. Instead of concentrating capabilities on response, it distributes them into anticipation. It invests in information, data integration, and early decision mechanisms. This shift does not eliminate the need for response, but reduces the likelihood that response will be insufficient.

Another central aspect is perception. A system that demonstrates anticipatory capacity reduces uncertainty. A system that reacts late increases it. Perception of control influences the behavior of economic and political actors. Maintaining that perception is part of protection.

The transition from a reactive model to a proactive one is not immediate. It requires changes in culture, processes, and decision structures. It requires accepting that the cost of acting early is lower than the cost of reacting late.

In an environment where events propagate rapidly, time becomes the scarcest resource.

And in that context, proactivity is not a strategic option.

It is a condition for survival.

Because in the global system, the winner is not the one who responds better.

It is the one who responds first.
