## Chapter 7

### Non-state actors

Piracy, militias, and asymmetric disruption

The global system is not controlled only by states.

It is also affected by actors that do not operate within formal structures.

Groups without defined territory.

Without institutional responsibilities.

Yet capable of generating impact.

These are non-state actors.

And their relevance in the chokepoint system is increasing.

Unlike states, these actors do not seek balance.

They do not seek stability.

They are not constrained by traditional rules.

Their logic is different.

They operate through asymmetry.

With limited resources.

But with clear objectives.

Maximize impact.

Minimize exposure.

That is the principle.

Chokepoints are particularly sensitive to these actors.

Because they concentrate flow.

Because they reduce maneuverability.

Because they amplify effects.

A small event in a constrained space can generate wide consequences.

That is the ideal environment for asymmetric disruption.

Piracy is the most visible form.

Attacks on vessels.

Interruption of transit.

Operational risk.

But its impact extends beyond the event.

It affects perception.

It increases costs.

It alters decisions.

Militias operate in a similar way.

They do not need to control large territories.

It is enough to influence strategic points.

Introduce uncertainty.

Alter conditions.

That is sufficient to affect the system.

Asymmetric disruption does not aim to fully block flow.

It aims to make it more expensive.

Slower.

More uncertain.

That is the mechanism.

Flow continues.

But under pressure.

The vulnerabilities to these actors are specific.

First, predictability.

Routes are known.

Critical points are defined.

Second, concentration.

Flow accumulates in limited spaces.

Third, dependence on continuity.

Any disruption generates impact.

Fourth, difficulty of response.

These actors do not follow traditional patterns.

They do not confront directly.

They operate in dispersed ways.

The impact of their actions is not linear.

A single isolated event can produce amplified effects.

Insurers adjust premiums.

Operators change routes.

Markets react.

The system responds.

Not only to the event.

But to the possibility of repetition.

From a strategic perspective, these actors introduce an additional variable.

Unpredictability.

It is not always possible to anticipate timing.

Or method.

That increases the complexity of protection.

Current capabilities allow response to events.

But anticipation is more difficult.

And within that gap lies risk.

A proactive doctrine in this context involves several elements.

First, continuous monitoring.

Identifying early signals.

Second, coordination among actors.

Sharing information.

Third, rapid response capacity.

Limiting initial impact.

Fourth, constant adaptation.

Adjusting strategies as conditions change.

These measures do not eliminate the threat.

But they reduce its effectiveness.

Non-state actors represent a key dimension of the modern system.

Where power is not concentrated.

But distributed.

Where the ability to generate impact does not depend on scale.

But on precision.

To understand this is to understand that risk does not come only from large events.

It comes from multiple small actions.

That, together, can alter the system.

Because in an interdependent environment, it is not necessary to stop the flow.

It is enough to disrupt it.

And within that disruption, often limited but persistent, lies the threat.
