## Chapter 8

### Hormuz: the switch of global oil

The global energy system is not distributed.

It is concentrated.

And nowhere is that concentration more evident than in the Strait of Hormuz.

A significant portion of the world’s oil supply passes through this narrow passage¹.

It is not just a route.

It is a mandatory transit point.

A place where energy does not flow by convenience.

It flows by necessity.

Hormuz connects the world’s primary oil-producing region with global markets.

Without that flow, the energy system loses continuity.

And when energy loses continuity, the impact is not sectoral.

It is systemic.

Oil is not just a commodity.

It is a foundational input.

It affects transportation.

Production.

Distribution.

Costs.

Inflation.

Stability.

That is why when flow through Hormuz is threatened, the system does not wait for a full disruption.

It reacts to the possibility.

Markets adjust prices.

Expectations shift.

Decisions accelerate.

That is the first level of impact.

Not physical.

Perceptual.

The second level is operational.

A chokepoint like Hormuz does not need to be completely closed to generate disruption.

It is enough to reduce capacity.

Increase uncertainty.

Raise perceived risk.

Ships are delayed.

Insurance premiums rise.

Routes are reconsidered.

Flow continues.

But under pressure.

And that pressure propagates.

To costs.

To timelines.

To stability.

The third level is strategic.

Hormuz is not only an energy passage.

It is a point of influence.

A location where state and non-state actors can alter the behavior of the global system through limited actions.

It does not require large-scale operations.

It requires precision.

A localized event.

A signal of risk.

A shift in perception.

That is sufficient to trigger chain reactions.

That is the defining characteristic of Hormuz.

Not its size.

Its effect.

The vulnerabilities of this chokepoint are clear.

First, concentration.

A high volume of flow in a constrained space.

Second, dependence.

Few alternatives with comparable capacity.

Third, proximity to geopolitical tension.

Fourth, the sensitivity of energy markets.

These factors make Hormuz a point of high exposure.

And of high strategic relevance.

Current capabilities allow for monitoring traffic and maintaining presence in the area.

But effective protection is not limited to observation.

It requires anticipation.

It requires understanding how small disturbances can generate large effects.

It requires acting before perceived risk becomes operational reality.

A proactive doctrine applied to Hormuz involves several elements.

First, full visibility of the flow.

Not only of vessels.

But of the conditions that affect their movement.

Second, early identification of signals.

Changes in regional behavior.

Increases in perceived risk.

Shifts in logistical patterns.

Third, rapid response mechanisms.

The ability to act before the system enters a phase of degradation.

Fourth, strengthening of alternatives.

Not to fully replace the flow.

But to reduce its concentration.

None of these measures eliminate vulnerability.

But all reduce its impact.

Hormuz cannot be understood as an isolated point.

It must be analyzed as part of a system.

A system in which energy defines the pace of the global economy.

And where any disruption, however small, has amplified consequences.

The true risk is not a total closure.

It is sustained uncertainty.

Because in global energy markets, uncertainty acts as a multiplier.

It increases prices.

Reduces predictability.

Accelerates decisions.

And generates effects that extend far beyond the point of origin.

Hormuz is, in essence, a switch.

Not because it controls the entire system.

But because it can alter it.

Within hours.

That is its power.

And that is its vulnerability.
