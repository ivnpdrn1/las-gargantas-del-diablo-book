## Chapter 26

### International coordination: what’s missing

The greatest strategic gap in the current system

The global system is interdependent.

Its management is not.

That is the problem.

Flows are global.

Decisions are not.

Each actor operates with its own logic.

Its own interests.

Its own priorities.

And within that fragmentation, the system loses response capacity.

International coordination is not optional.

It is a condition.

Without it, resilience is limited.

Partial.

Inefficient.

The system can function.

But it cannot fully protect itself.

That is the strategic gap.

The first problem is the absence of a common framework.

There are agreements.

There are institutions.

But there is no integrated architecture.

No structure that enables coherent action in the face of disruption.

The second problem is divergence of interests.

What is critical for one country may not be for another.

Priorities do not always align.

And that difference complicates collective action.

The third problem is speed.

The global system operates in real time.

International coordination does not.

Decisions require negotiation.

Alignment.

Time.

And that time amplifies impact.

The fourth problem is information.

Not all actors have access to the same data.

Not all share it.

That limits visibility.

And without visibility, coordination is incomplete.

The fifth problem is trust.

Cooperation depends on it.

And it is not always present.

Geopolitical tensions weaken the ability to act jointly.

These problems are not new.

But their impact is greater in an interdependent system.

Because lack of coordination does not only limit response.

It amplifies risk.

From a structural perspective, the global system functions as a network.

But it is managed as separate parts.

That mismatch is the vulnerability.

From an adversarial perspective, fragmentation is an opportunity.

A system that does not act in coordination is easier to pressure.

Easier to divide.

Easier to disrupt.

There is no need to create a large-scale event.

It is enough to exploit misalignment.

That is the mechanism.

Current capabilities allow cooperation at certain levels.

Information sharing.

Targeted agreements.

But they are not sufficient.

Coordination remains reactive.

Not structural.

An effective architecture requires progress on several fronts.

First, establishing common frameworks.

Shared principles.

Second, improving information integration.

Accessible data.

A shared view.

Third, accelerating decision mechanisms.

Reducing response time.

Fourth, strengthening trust.

The foundation of cooperation.

Fifth, institutionalizing coordination.

Not relying only on ad hoc initiatives.

These measures do not eliminate differences.

But they make them manageable.

International coordination does not mean uniformity.

It means coherence.

The ability to act together when the system requires it.

The greatest risk is not disruption itself.

It is the inability to respond in a coordinated way.

Because in an interdependent system, no action is isolated.

And no response should be either.

Global resilience is not built within a single country.

It is built through interaction.

And within that interaction, there is currently a gap.

Understanding that gap is the first step.

Closing it is the challenge.

And within that challenge lies the future of the global system.
