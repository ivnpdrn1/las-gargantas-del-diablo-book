## Chapter 29

### The next crisis will not be local

Why risk is systemic

The global system has changed.

But the way risk is understood has not evolved at the same pace.

For decades, crises were perceived as localized events.

A conflict.

A disruption.

A specific failure.

And the response followed that logic.

Contain.

Isolate.

Restore.

But that model is no longer sufficient.

The current system does not allow true isolation.

Everything is connected.

Flows.

Markets.

Decisions.

Expectations.

That interconnection redefines risk.

It makes it systemic.

The next crisis will not begin as global.

It will begin as local.

As it always has.

But it will not remain so.

It will propagate.

Rapidly.

Across multiple layers.

That is the critical point.

Scale is not defined by origin.

It is defined by propagation.

An event in an energy chokepoint affects global prices.

A logistical disruption alters supply chains.

A technological constraint impacts production.

A shift in food flows generates social pressure.

Everything connects.

Everything amplifies.

The system does not fail at a single point.

It fails in the interaction between points.

That is the structural shift.

From an operational perspective, this means there is no perfect containment.

Barriers are temporary.

Partial.

Impact finds pathways.

It moves.

It adapts.

From a financial perspective, propagation is immediate.

Markets react in real time.

Risk perception expands.

Decisions adjust.

Impact amplifies.

From a social perspective, consequences are cumulative.

Inflation.

Scarcity.

Uncertainty.

These factors generate pressure.

And that pressure transcends economies.

It affects stability.

From a strategic perspective, this redefines response.

It is not about reacting to a single event.

It is about managing a chain.

A sequence of impacts.

Unfolding in parallel.

From an adversarial perspective, systemic risk is exploitable.

A large-scale event is not required.

Multiple smaller events.

Across different points.

Can generate greater impact.

That is the mechanism.

Distribute pressure.

Activate the network.

The system amplifies the effect.

Current capabilities are designed for isolated events.

But today’s risk is multiple.

Interconnected.

Dynamic.

That is the challenge.

An effective doctrine requires a shift in approach.

First, systemic vision.

Understanding interdependencies.

Second, simultaneous monitoring.

Not one point.

But many.

Third, global coordination.

Response must be integrated.

Fourth, adaptive capacity.

Responding in real time.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they make it manageable.

The next crisis will not be local.

Even if it begins that way.

It will be global in its effects.

And systemic in its nature.

Understanding this is essential.

Because risk no longer resides in the event.

It resides in the network.

And in that network, every connection matters.

The global system does not collapse from a single cause.

It degrades through many.

And within that degradation, gradual but persistent, the crisis unfolds.

The challenge is not preventing the first event.

It is preventing it from becoming a chain.

Because once the chain is activated, control diminishes.

And impact amplifies.

That is the new risk.

And the new reality.
