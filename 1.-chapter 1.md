## Chapter 1

### The World Is Not Flat: It Is Vulnerable

The world does not function as we believe.

For decades, the dominant narrative has been clear: we live in a global economy that is connected, distributed, and resilient.

A network.

A system where everything is interconnected and, therefore, protected by its own complexity.

But that idea is incomplete.

And in some respects, dangerous.

The global system is not a balanced network.

It is an uneven structure.

A system where flow is not distributed evenly, but concentrated in specific points.

Points that, while small in geographic scale, sustain disproportionately large functions.

These points are not always visible.

They do not appear in public debate with the frequency their importance would justify.

Yet their impact is total.

The energy that powers industries, the trade that sustains economies, and the goods that move across continents depend on highly concentrated global routes¹.

And those routes are not infinite.

They narrow.

They concentrate.

They become vulnerable.

It is in those places that the system reveals its true nature.

Not as a distributed network, but as a series of critical dependencies.

A structure where stability does not come from redundancy, but from continuity.

The problem is not the existence of these points.

All infrastructure has them.

The problem is how they are understood.

It is assumed that because they have worked, they will continue to work.

It is assumed that past continuity guarantees future continuity.

But complex systems do not operate under that logic.

A system can function for years without failing.

And still be one point away from doing so.

The concept of vulnerability in this context is not obvious.

It is not about visible fragility.

It is not about an obvious structural weakness.

It is about concentration.

When a critical function depends on a limited number of points, the system does not become more efficient.

It becomes more exposed².

Efficiency, in this case, carries a hidden cost.

It reduces redundancy.
It optimizes routes.
It minimizes time.

But it also removes margins for error.

This is not an accidental flaw.

It is a consequence of design.

The global system has been optimized to operate under normal conditions.

Not to withstand adverse ones³.

And that difference is fundamental.

Because adverse conditions do not need to be extreme to generate impact.

They only need to occur in the right place.

A delay in a secondary route is an inconvenience.

A disruption at a critical point is a systemic event.

The difference is not in the magnitude of the event.

It is in its location.

The world, then, is not flat.

It is not homogeneous.

It is not uniformly resilient.

It is uneven.

And that unevenness defines its vulnerability.

Some points matter more than others.

Some locations sustain more than they appear to.

Some spaces, if altered, affect far beyond their geography.

These places are the chokepoints of the global system.

They are not merely narrow passages.

They are points of decision.

Places where flow can be sustained or interrupted.

And that characteristic turns them into more than logistical elements.

It turns them into strategic instruments.

Those who understand these points understand the system.

Those who ignore them overestimate its stability.

Vulnerability is not distributed.

It is concentrated.

And understanding that concentration is the first step toward protecting it.

This book begins with that idea.

That the world is not an infinite network of secure connections.

It is a system of flows that depends on specific points.

Points where continuity can be sustained.

Or broken.

Because ultimately, global stability does not depend on everything.

It depends on a few.

And those few are enough to change everything.
