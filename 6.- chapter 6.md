## Chapter 6

### Failures of the Current System

What exists… and what does not

The global system does not fail because of a lack of infrastructure.

It fails because of a lack of preparation.

For decades, the protection of chokepoints has been built on an implicit assumption: the system will continue to function as it always has.

That assumption is the greatest risk.

Today, significant capabilities exist.

Maritime traffic monitoring.

Control of strategic routes.

Naval presence in critical areas.

Operational management of key passages such as Suez.

But these capabilities are not designed for sustained disruption.

They are designed for stability.

And that is where the failure lies.

The system observes.

But it does not always anticipate.

The system reacts.

But it does not always arrive in time.

The system coordinates.

But not always with the speed required.

The difference between capability and effectiveness is not technical.

It is structural.

In theory, the world’s main corridors are under constant surveillance.

In practice, that surveillance does not always translate into actionable decisions.

Information exists.

But it is not always integrated.

Data is available.

But it is not always connected.

Actors are present.

But they are not always aligned.

This is the first level of failure.

Not the absence of resources.

But the fragmentation of them.

The second level of failure is the dependence on reaction.

The dominant model assumes that the event occurs first.

And the response follows.

In highly interdependent systems, that sequence is problematic.

By the time the response is activated, the impact has already begun to spread.

Markets react.

Routes adjust.

Costs increase.

And the system enters a phase of degradation.

Not because of the event itself.

But because of the delay in response.

The third level of failure is the illusion of redundancy.

In many cases, it is assumed that alternatives exist.

Longer routes.

Additional logistical capacity.

Complementary energy options.

But these alternatives are not designed to absorb large-scale shifts in flow.

They are designed as complements.

Not as substitutes.

When activated simultaneously, they generate additional pressure.

More congestion.

Higher costs.

Greater uncertainty.

Redundancy exists.

But it is not sufficient.

The fourth level of failure is the lack of real coordination.

Protecting chokepoints involves multiple actors.

States.

Private companies.

Logistics operators.

International institutions.

Each with its own interests.

Its own timelines.

Its own priorities.

Without clear mechanisms for joint decision-making, response becomes fragmented.

And when response fragments, it loses effectiveness.

The fifth level of failure is dependence on confidence.

The global system functions because actors believe it will continue to function.

That confidence reduces costs.

Accelerates decisions.

Optimizes flows.

But it also creates vulnerability.

When confidence is affected, the system changes its behavior.

Before any actual disruption occurs.

Operators reroute.

Insurers adjust premiums.

Markets price in risk.

The impact begins before the event.

That is the critical point.

The system does not need to fail to generate consequences.

It only needs to be perceived as unstable.

To understand these failures, it is not necessary to rely on hypothetical scenarios.

It is enough to observe existing corridors.

The Bab el-Mandeb–Red Sea–Suez axis provides a clear example.

A corridor where large volumes of trade converge.

Where efficiency has reduced costs.

And where any disruption produces immediate effects.

It is not necessary to fully close the canal.

It is not necessary to block access.

It is enough to introduce uncertainty.

The system responds automatically.

Flows are diverted.

Transit times increase.

Costs rise.

Disruption does not occur as a sudden collapse.

It occurs as progressive degradation.

And that degradation is enough to generate global impact.

This pattern is not an exception.

It is a feature of the system.

A system designed for maximum efficiency tends to minimize redundancy.

And in doing so, increases its exposure.

The current failures are not accidental.

They are the result of accumulated decisions.

Optimization over resilience.

Speed over margin for error.

Cost over redundancy.

Correcting these failures does not require rebuilding the system from scratch.

It requires recognizing its limits.

And acting before those limits turn into crises.

Because in an interdependent system, the question is not whether disruption will occur.

It is when.

And the difference between impact and collapse lies in what is done before it happens.
