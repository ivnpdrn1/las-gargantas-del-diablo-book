## Chapter 6

### Hybrid chokepoints

Cyberattacks, insurance, sanctions, and economic warfare

Not all chokepoints are geographic.

Some have no coordinates.

They cannot be pointed to on a map.

Yet they can stop the system.

These are hybrid chokepoints.

Points of control where flow is not physically blocked, but functionally disrupted.

Where the system does not disappear.

But it no longer operates normally.

The global system depends on more than routes.

It depends on processes.

It depends on permissions.

It depends on trust.

And these elements can be altered without physically blocking a passage.

That is the fundamental shift.

Disruption no longer requires physical presence.

It can be executed remotely.

Through digital systems.

Through regulatory structures.

Through financial decisions.

A cyberattack can halt port operations.

It can disrupt navigation systems.

It can interrupt logistical coordination.

It does not block the route.

But it stops the flow.

Maritime insurance is another critical point.

Without coverage, many vessels will not sail.

Not because they cannot.

But because they cannot afford the risk.

An increase in premiums.

A restriction of coverage.

And flow declines.

Sanctions operate in a similar way.

They do not block routes.

They block actors.

They limit access to markets.

To financial systems.

To infrastructure.

The result is the same.

Less flow.

More friction.

Economic warfare does not require the destruction of infrastructure.

It requires altering conditions.

Making operations more expensive.

More uncertain.

More complex.

And within that complexity, the system loses efficiency.

Hybrid chokepoints share a key characteristic.

They are not visible.

They do not generate images.

They do not produce immediate physical events.

But their impact is real.

And often harder to contain.

The vulnerabilities in this domain are different.

First, digital dependence.

Interconnected systems that can be affected remotely.

Second, financial dependence.

Flow depends on economic conditions.

Third, regulatory dependence.

Rules that can change.

Fourth, speed of propagation.

Decisions in these systems are immediate.

The impact of a hybrid disruption does not appear as a single event.

It appears as a shift in conditions.

The system continues.

But under new constraints.

Slower.

More expensive.

More uncertain.

From an adversarial perspective, this expands the field of action.

There is no need to act on geography.

One can act on function.

On the systems that enable flow.

That is the critical point.

Flow does not depend only on space.

It depends on the system that sustains it.

And that system can be altered.

Current capabilities allow detection of some of these threats.

But effective protection requires a different approach.

It is not enough to protect routes.

Processes must be protected.

Systems must be protected.

Decisions must be protected.

A proactive doctrine in this context involves several elements.

First, digital resilience.

The ability to maintain operations under cyber pressure.

Second, financial redundancy.

Mechanisms that allow continuity even under economic constraints.

Third, regulatory coordination.

Avoiding fragmented rules.

Fourth, signal monitoring.

Changes in behavior.

System anomalies.

Indicators of pressure.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they manage it.

Hybrid chokepoints represent the evolution of the system.

Where control is not exercised only over space.

But over conditions.

To understand this is to understand that vulnerability is no longer purely physical.

It is systemic.

And within that system, invisible yet critical, lies the new domain of risk.

Because in today’s world, it is not necessary to close a passage to stop the flow.

It is enough to make it function worse.

And within that degradation, often silent, lies disruption.
