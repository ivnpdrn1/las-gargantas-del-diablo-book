## Chapter 21

### If Taiwan is blocked

The industrial collapse of the 21st century

The global system can adapt to logistical disruptions.

It can reroute flows.

It can absorb costs.

It can reconfigure supply chains.

But there is a limit.

Productive capacity.

If Taiwan is blocked, the impact is not only on transit.

It is on production.

And that distinction is fundamental.

Because at this point, it is not only trade that flows.

Capacity is concentrated.

A significant share of global semiconductor production is located in Taiwan¹.

Essential components.

Irreplaceable in the short term.

Without them, the modern economy loses continuity.

The first effect is industrial.

Factories cannot operate without inputs.

Automotive production.

Electronics.

Industrial equipment.

All depend on these components.

Production slows.

Or stops.

Not because of lack of demand.

But because of lack of capacity.

The second effect is technological.

Development decelerates.

Projects are delayed.

Innovation loses momentum.

The digital economy is affected.

The third effect is economic.

Scarcity drives price increases.

Products become more expensive.

Markets react.

Uncertainty rises.

The fourth effect is systemic.

There are no immediate alternatives.

Production cannot be relocated quickly.

Supply chains are complex.

Interdependent.

The impact is not localized.

It is global.

The fifth effect is strategic.

Technology becomes a critical resource.

Policy decisions accelerate.

Relationships among actors shift.

The system enters a phase of redefinition.

These effects are not isolated.

They overlap.

They amplify each other.

And they produce a prolonged disruption.

Unlike other chokepoints, where impact can be managed in the short term, here the impact extends over time.

Because rebuilding capacity is not immediate.

From an adversarial perspective, a full blockade is not required.

It is enough to generate uncertainty.

To affect continuity.

To introduce risk.

That alone is sufficient to alter system behavior.

Companies adjust strategies.

They diversify suppliers.

They increase inventories.

Markets react.

Impact begins before full disruption.

That is the mechanism.

Not only blockade.

But loss of confidence in continuity.

Current capabilities allow monitoring of transit and production.

But effective protection requires a different approach.

It is not only about securing routes.

It is about securing capacity.

A proactive doctrine involves several elements.

First, diversification of production.

Reducing concentration.

Second, development of alternative capabilities.

Investing in new locations.

Third, international coordination.

The impact is global.

The response must be as well.

Fourth, monitoring of signals.

Changes in the political environment.

Variations in production.

Indicators of risk.

These measures do not eliminate vulnerability.

But they reduce it.

If Taiwan is blocked, the global system does not collapse immediately.

But it loses its ability to sustain pace.

It slows.

It becomes more expensive.

It becomes uncertain.

And in that process, it is redefined.

Because in the 21st century, the economy does not depend only on energy or transport.

It depends on technology.

And when that technology is concentrated in a single location, risk is no longer logistical.

It becomes structural.
