## Chapter 16

### The Danish Straits: the militarized Baltic

Not all chokepoints lie in open waters.

Some exist within enclosed spaces.

And in those spaces, control intensifies.

The Danish Straits—Skagerrak, Kattegat, and Øresund—connect the Baltic Sea to the North Sea.

They are the entry and exit points of the Baltic.

A mandatory passage.

A system where access is not optional.

It is structural.

Through these straits flow energy.

Goods.

Regional and international trade.

But above all, interdependence.

The Baltic states, Scandinavia, and parts of Europe rely on this connection.

Without it, the Baltic becomes an enclosed system.

And when a system becomes enclosed, its dynamics change.

The impact of these chokepoints is not defined only by volume.

It is defined by context.

The Baltic is a region of high strategic sensitivity.

A space where military, energy, and commercial interests converge.

That convergence amplifies any disturbance.

The vulnerabilities of the Danish Straits are specific.

First, concentration of access.

A limited set of passages connects an entire region to the global system.

Second, proximity of actors with divergent interests.

This introduces tension.

Third, militarization of the environment.

The presence of military capabilities alters the dynamics of flow.

Fourth, energy dependence.

A portion of the region’s energy supply passes through this system.

The impact of disruption does not appear as immediate collapse.

It appears as pressure.

Flow continues.

But under different conditions.

Increased monitoring.

Restrictions.

Uncertainty.

And that uncertainty affects decisions.

Routes shift.

Costs increase.

From a strategic perspective, these straits do not need to be closed.

It is enough to change the environment.

Increase tension.

Alter perception.

The system responds.

Actors adjust behavior.

Decisions become more conservative.

That is the mechanism.

Not interruption.

But pressure.

From an adversarial perspective, this opens multiple scenarios.

There is no need to act directly on the flow.

One can act on the context.

Create instability.

Introduce risk.

That alone is sufficient to alter the system.

Current capabilities allow for monitoring and managing transit.

But effective protection requires more than operational control.

It requires strategic stability.

It requires coordination among allies.

It requires clarity in rules of interaction.

A proactive doctrine in this context involves several elements.

First, full visibility of the environment.

Not only of traffic.

But of the conditions affecting it.

Second, coordination among regional actors.

Response cannot be fragmented.

Third, monitoring of pressure signals.

Changes in military activity.

Variations in navigation patterns.

Shifts in energy flows.

Fourth, preparation for escalation scenarios.

Not waiting for them to occur.

But anticipating them.

These measures do not eliminate risk.

But they reduce its impact.

The Danish Straits represent an evolution of the chokepoint concept.

Where geography intersects with strategy.

Where flow depends as much on physical space as on the political and military environment.

To understand this system is to understand that risk does not always come from direct disruption.

It often comes from the surrounding environment.

And within that environment, even small changes can produce significant effects.

Because in an interdependent system, it is not necessary to block a passage to generate impact.

It is enough to alter the conditions under which that passage operates.

And within that alteration, often subtle, lies vulnerability.
